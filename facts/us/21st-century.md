## 1. The 2000 Election (2000)

### The Vote and the Crisis

The 2000 presidential election between Republican George W. Bush and Democrat Al Gore was decided by one of the most controversial Supreme Court decisions in American history.

**Key Facts:**
- On November 8, 2000, the initial vote tally in Florida showed Bush leading by approximately 1,700 votes
- Florida's 25 electoral votes would determine the presidency
- The margin was less than 0.5%, triggering an automatic machine recount under Florida law
- After the first recount, Bush's lead shrank to just 317 votes
- On November 26, Secretary of State Katherine Harris certified Bush as the winner by 537 votes

*Sources: [Bush v. Gore - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bush_v._Gore); [Britannica - Bush v. Gore](https://www.britannica.com/event/Bush-v-Gore)*

### Conflicts of Interest

The election was overseen by officials with direct ties to the Bush campaign:
- **Jeb Bush**, George W. Bush's brother, was Governor of Florida
- **Katherine Harris**, Florida's Secretary of State, was co-chair of Bush's Florida campaign
- Florida Attorney General Bob Butterworth headed the Gore campaign

*Source: [Miller Center - Bush v. Gore](https://millercenter.org/contested-presidential-elections/bush-v-gore)*

### Bush v. Gore Supreme Court Decision

On December 12, 2000, the Supreme Court issued its ruling:
- **7-2**: Found Florida's inconsistent recount process violated the Equal Protection Clause
- **5-4**: Ruled there was no time for a constitutionally valid recount before the Electoral College deadline
- The majority opinion included unusual language: the decision "should be limited to the present circumstances" and not set precedent

The five conservative justices (Rehnquist, O'Connor, Scalia, Kennedy, Thomas) voted to stop the recount, effectively handing the election to Bush.

**Aftermath:**
- Bush won the Electoral College 271-266
- Gore won the national popular vote by approximately 500,000 votes
- The Congressional Black Caucus protested during the official Electoral College count
- Justice Sandra Day O'Connor later expressed regret, saying in 2013: "Maybe the court should have said, 'We're not going to take it, goodbye.'"

*Sources: [Constitution Center - Bush v. Gore](https://constitutioncenter.org/blog/on-this-day-bush-v-gore-anniversary); [History.com - 2000 Election](https://www.history.com/articles/2000-election-bush-gore-votes-supreme-court)*

### Voter Suppression: The Purge List

**The Felon Purge Program:**
In 1999, under newly elected Governor Jeb Bush and Secretary of State Katherine Harris, Florida contracted with Database Technologies Inc. (DBT) to create a master list of former felons to remove from voter rolls.

**Critical Flaws:**
- Names were flagged with only a **70% match** between voter rolls and felon databases
- DBT warned Harris's office in writing that their criteria would include many non-felons
- Harris's office instructed them to **lower** the match criteria further to 85%
- The final list contained **82,389 voters** flagged for removal

**Racial Impact:**
- Black Floridians made up only **11%** of registered voters
- But they comprised **44%** of those on the purge list
- Many flagged voters were not felons at all

**Verification Failures:**
- In Leon County, officials tried to verify 694 names from the Tallahassee list
- Only 34 were actual matches - a **5% accuracy rate**
- In May 2000, 8,000 voters were purged based on a Texas felon list - but none had committed felonies, only misdemeanors

**Impact Analysis:**
The US Civil Rights Commission investigated. Their general counsel, Edward Hailes, calculated:
- If 12,000 voters were wrongly purged
- And 44% were African-American
- And 90% of African-Americans voted for Gore
- Then approximately **4,752 Black Gore voters** were prevented from voting
- This was nearly **nine times** Bush's margin of victory

The Commission's June 2001 report found "strong and credible evidence of violations of Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act."

*Sources: [US Civil Rights Commission Report](https://www.usccr.gov/files/pubs/vote2000/report/exesum.htm); [The Nation - Florida Voter Disenfranchisement](https://www.thenation.com/article/archive/how-the-2000-election-in-florida-led-to-a-new-wave-of-voter-disenfranchisement/); [Salon - Florida's Voter Cleansing](https://www.salon.com/2000/12/04/voter_file/)*

## 2. 9/11 and the Surveillance State (2001)

### The USA PATRIOT Act

On September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks killed nearly 3,000 Americans. The government response fundamentally transformed American civil liberties.

**The PATRIOT Act:**
- Passed **45 days** after 9/11 (October 26, 2001)
- The 131-page law was enacted without amendment and with little dissent, **three days** after its introduction
- Many provisions were items on a "longstanding law enforcement wish list that had been previously rejected by Congress"

**Expanded Powers:**
- **Section 215**: Government can obtain "any tangible things" (including library records, medical records) for terrorism investigations
- **Section 206**: Roving wiretaps covering multiple devices without individual authorizations
- **Secret searches**: Expanded ability to search private property without notice
- **Reduced oversight**: Weakened judicial oversight, public accountability, and ability to challenge searches

*Sources: [ACLU - End Mass Surveillance](https://www.aclu.org/end-mass-surveillance-under-the-patriot-act); [Britannica - USA PATRIOT Act](https://www.britannica.com/topic/USA-PATRIOT-Act); [Brennan Center - Rolling Back Surveillance State](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/rolling-back-post-911-surveillance-state)*

### Transformation of Surveillance

**From Warrants to Mass Collection:**
The PATRIOT Act transformed the legal framework "from one that requires the government to obtain a warrant when acquiring Americans' most sensitive data to one that allows the government to amass such information without any suspicion of wrongdoing whatsoever."

**Constitutional Challenges:**
Over the years, multiple provisions of the PATRIOT Act were challenged in court and declared unconstitutional as violations of the Fourth Amendment.

**Lasting Infrastructure:**
Despite these rulings, the surveillance infrastructure created by the PATRIOT Act "exists to this day" and has been "renewed repeatedly by successive administrations and Congresses controlled by both parties."

*Sources: [Bill of Rights Institute - USA PATRIOT Act](https://www.billofrightsinstitute.org/essays/the-usa-patriot-act); [EPIC - PATRIOT Act](https://epic.org/issues/surveillance-oversight/patriot-act/)*

### Post-9/11 Islamophobia

**Hate Crime Surge:**
- Hate crimes against Muslims rose **1,617%** from 2000 to 2001
- 481 reported incidents in 2001 vs. 28 in 2000
- Hate crimes against Muslims spiked **500%** from 2000-2009 overall
- Numbers have **never returned to pre-9/11 levels**

**Immediate Violence:**
- Mosques were burned or destroyed
- Death threats and harassment followed many Muslims
- On September 15, 2001, Balbir Singh Sodhi, a Sikh American in Mesa, Arizona, was murdered - one of the first post-9/11 hate crimes (Sikhs were targeted because of turbans)
- The Sikh Coalition documented over **300 cases** of anti-Sikh violence and discrimination in the first months after 9/11

**Government Profiling:**
- Anti-racial profiling legislation pending before Congress was abandoned
- The FBI developed "ethnic mapping" and infiltrated mosques
- Informants were tasked with relaying attendees' conversations
- People were singled out at airports and borders for appearing Muslim, Arab, or South Asian

**Lasting Impact:**
- A 2002 poll found only 25% of Americans viewed Islam as dangerous
- By later years, 50% surveyed said Islam was more likely than other religions to encourage violence
- Hate crimes against Muslims remain **5 times more common** than before 9/11

*Sources: [Al Jazeera - Muslims Battle Islamophobia](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2022/9/11/decades-after-9-11-muslims-battle-islamophobia-in); [DOJ - Combating Post-9/11 Backlash](https://www.justice.gov/crt/combating-post-911-discriminatory-backlash-6); [ABC News - 20 Years of Islamophobia](https://abcnews.go.com/US/20-years-911-islamophobia-continues-haunt-muslims/story?id=79732049)*

## 3. The Afghanistan War (2001-2021)

### Overview

The Afghanistan War became the **longest war in American history**, spanning 20 years across four presidencies.

**Origins:**
- On September 20, 2001, President Bush demanded the Taliban deliver Osama bin Laden and al Qaeda leaders "or share in their fate"
- On October 7, 2001, US and British forces launched Operation Enduring Freedom
- With Northern Alliance help, US forces overtook Taliban strongholds including Kabul by mid-November 2001

*Sources: [History.com - Afghanistan War](https://www.history.com/articles/afghanistan-war); [George W. Bush Library - War in Afghanistan](https://www.georgewbushlibrary.gov/research/topic-guides/the-war-in-afghanistan)*

### Financial Cost

**Total Expenditure: Approximately $2.3 trillion**
- Brown University's Costs of War Project estimates the true cost closer to **$2.26 trillion** when including future veteran care and interest on war borrowing
- This equals approximately **$136 million per day** for nearly 20 years
- Approximately **$25,000 for each** of the 40 million people living in Afghanistan

*Sources: [Brown University Costs of War](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/paper/afghanistan-and-after-20-years-war-2001-2021); [Fortune - Cost of Afghanistan War](https://fortune.com/2021/08/18/cost-of-afghanistan-war-time-casualties-deaths-money/)*

### Human Casualties

**US and Allied Forces:**
- **2,324** American military personnel killed
- **3,917** military contractors killed
- **1,144** allied troops killed
- **20,722** US service members wounded in action
- **980,000** US Afghanistan war veterans (507,000 served in both Afghanistan and Iraq)

**Afghan Casualties:**
- At least **46,000 Afghan civilians** killed
- **70,000+ military and law enforcement** personnel killed
- Civilian deaths were higher in the war's latter years, with 2015 and 2016 both breaking annual records

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Afghanistan War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%932021)); [The Conversation - Costs of Afghanistan War](https://theconversation.com/calculating-the-costs-of-the-afghanistan-war-in-lives-dollars-and-years-164588)*

### The Taliban's Return (2021)

**Key Events:**
- On May 2, 2011, Osama bin Laden was killed in Pakistan
- On February 29, 2020, the US and Taliban signed a peace agreement (though Taliban attacks continued)
- In April 2021, President Biden set September 11, 2021 as the full withdrawal deadline

**The Collapse:**
- In just **10 days** (August 6-15, 2021), the Taliban swept through the country
- Provincial capitals fell with little resistance
- Kabul fell on August 15, 2021
- The last US forces departed on August 30, 2021

**Aftermath:**
"The Taliban quickly regained control of the country, undoing many of the political and social reforms that resulted from American intervention."

*Sources: [CFR - US War in Afghanistan Timeline](https://www.cfr.org/timeline/us-war-afghanistan); [Wikipedia - War in Afghanistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%932021))*

## 4. The Iraq War (2003-2011)

### WMD: The Intelligence Failure

**The Case for War:**
The primary rationale for the 2003 invasion was the assessment that Saddam Hussein possessed chemical and biological weapons and was pursuing nuclear weapons.

**What Actually Happened:**
- "No trace of nonconventional weapons was ever found in Iraq"
- The President's Commission on WMD Intelligence called this "one of the most public - the most damaging - intelligence failures in recent American history"
- Inspectors concluded "prior to the war there had been no operational WMDs in Iraq because their production had ceased after the 1991 Gulf War"

**How It Went Wrong:**
- "An analytical process that was driven by assumptions and inferences rather than data"
- Critical source "Curveball" was never interviewed by American intelligence until after the war
- Curveball was handled exclusively by German intelligence, who regarded his statements as unconvincing
- The October 2002 National Intelligence Estimate concluding Iraq "has" biological weapons was "based almost exclusively" on Curveball's information

**What Saddam Actually Said:**
Iraqi archives reveal Saddam "consistently and repeatedly conveyed the truth to his underlings about Iraq's lack of illicit weapons programs." In one closed-door meeting: "You might think we still have hidden chemical weapons, missiles and so forth. We have nothing; not even one screw."

*Sources: [Washington Post - Iraq WMDs](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2019/03/22/iraq-war-wmds-an-intelligence-failure-or-white-house-spin/); [War on the Rocks - Iraq Intelligence Failures](https://warontherocks.com/2023/03/the-iraq-wars-intelligence-failures-are-still-misunderstood/); [Wikipedia - Iraq and WMD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iraq_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction)*

### Casualty Estimates

Estimates of Iraqi casualties vary dramatically depending on methodology:

**Body Count Estimates (Direct Documentation):**
- Iraq Body Count documents **186,901-210,296** violent civilian deaths
- NBC News reported approximately **200,000** Iraqi civilians killed

**Survey-Based Estimates:**
- Iraq Family Health Survey (2006): **151,000** violent deaths
- PLOS Medicine (2013): **461,000** total deaths (60%+ violent) through June 2011
- 2006 Lancet study: **655,000** total deaths (90%+ violent) through June 2006
- ORB Survey (2008, highest estimate): **1,033,000** deaths (range: 946,000-1,120,000)

**Why Estimates Differ:**
- Population-based surveys extrapolate from random samples
- Body counts only tally reported deaths and "likely significantly underestimate casualties"
- Iraq Body Count notes "gaps in recording and reporting suggest that even our highest totals to date may be missing many civilian deaths"

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Casualties of the Iraq War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casualties_of_the_Iraq_War); [Brown University - Human Costs](https://costsofwar.watson.brown.edu/costs/human); [Iraq Body Count](https://www.iraqbodycount.org/)*

### Abu Ghraib: Torture

**Background:**
Abu Ghraib was reopened by the US military in August 2003 as a detention site. The US "began a mass round-up of Iraqi civilians - the vast majority of whom were never charged with any crime."

**The Scandal:**
- First reported by Associated Press in November 2003
- In January 2004, photos were discovered showing Iraqi prisoners being humiliated and tortured
- On April 28, 2004, "60 Minutes II" broadcast the graphic photos
- On April 30, 2004, The New Yorker published Seymour Hersh's report with Taguba investigation details

**Documented Abuses:**
Major General Taguba's report found "numerous incidents of sadistic, blatant, and wanton criminal abuses" including:
- Physical and sexual abuse, including rape
- Electric shock and mock executions
- Shackling in painful positions
- Sleep deprivation and isolation
- Extreme temperatures
- Exploitation of personal phobias
- Photography of nude detainees
- Use of military dogs

**Scale of Evidence:**
A report accessed by Salon documented:
- **1,325 images** of suspected detainee abuse
- **93 video files** of suspected detainee abuse
- **546 images** of suspected dead Iraqi detainees
- **37 images** of military dogs used in abuse

**Accountability:**
- 11 US soldiers were charged; 9 were sentenced to prison
- Private Lynndie England received 3 years (released after half)
- At least one inmate, Manadel al-Jamadi, died from abuse
- The US military itself has not paid any compensation to detainees

**Legal Settlements:**
- 2013: Engility Holdings paid **$5.28 million** to 71 former inmates
- November 2024: A US jury ordered defense contractor CACI to pay **$42 million** to three torture victims

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Abu Ghraib](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Ghraib_torture_and_prisoner_abuse); [Al Jazeera - Abu Ghraib Explained](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2024/11/14/what-happened-in-abu-ghraib-and-why-did-a-us-court-award-damages); [Center for Constitutional Rights - Abu Ghraib Factsheet](https://ccrjustice.org/home/get-involved/tools-resources/fact-sheets-and-faqs/factsheet-torture-abu-ghraib-and-al-shimari-v)*

## 5. Hurricane Katrina (2005)

### The Disaster and Response

On August 29, 2005, Hurricane Katrina struck New Orleans - the deadliest hurricane to hit the US mainland in 50 years.

**Federal Response Failure:**
- President Bush was on an extended vacation at his Texas ranch when Katrina made landfall
- His staff "reportedly declined to let him know the full extent of the damage"
- A photograph of Bush observing destruction from Air Force One windows became symbolic of perceived detachment
- FEMA Secretary Chertoff testified that FEMA was "overwhelmed" and acknowledged "many lapses"

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Criticism of Katrina Response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticism_of_the_government_response_to_Hurricane_Katrina); [Foreign Policy - Remembering Katrina](https://foreignpolicy.com/2025/08/27/hurricane-katrina-george-w-bush-political-aftermath/)*

### Racial Disparities in Deaths

**Death Toll by Demographics:**
- **51%** of Louisiana Katrina victims were Black
- **49%** were 75 years or older
- In Orleans Parish, the death rate for African-Americans was **1.7-4 times higher** than for whites
- Areas with the largest number of dead were "low-lying, flood-prone areas with predominantly Black populations"

**Public Opinion Divide:**
A 2005 Pew Poll found:
- **77%** of Black respondents said the government did a "fair or poor job" (vs. 55% of whites)
- **66%** of Black respondents believed the response "would have been faster if most victims had been white"
- Only **17%** of white Americans agreed

*Sources: [CNN - Katrina and Kanye West](https://www.cnn.com/2024/10/13/entertainment/kanye-katrina-tv-on-the-edge/index.html); [NPR - George Bush Doesn't Care 20 Years Later](https://www.npr.org/2025/08/27/nx-s1-5517328/george-bush-doesnt-care-about-black-people-20-years-later)*

### "George Bush Doesn't Care About Black People"

On September 2, 2005, four days after Katrina hit, NBC held "A Concert for Hurricane Relief" that drew 8.5 million viewers and raised $50 million.

**The Moment:**
Kanye West went off script during the telethon:
> "I hate the way they portray us in the media. If you see a Black family, it says they're looting. If you see a White family, it says they're looking for food."

He concluded: **"George Bush doesn't care about Black people."**

**Impact:**
- Bush later called it "one of the most disgusting moments of my presidency" and the "all-time low" point
- CNN's Van Jones described the moment as "part of the groundwork of what would come to be the Black Lives Matter movement"
- "It was a cathartic moment that this new generation was coming on the scene and they were going to call it like it is"
- Katrina "is widely seen as a pivot point in his presidency, sending his approval ratings plunging"

*Sources: [Slate - How Kanye's Moment Went Down](https://slate.com/news-and-politics/2015/08/george-bush-doesnt-care-about-black-people-how-kanye-west-s-katrina-moment-went-down-in-the-nbc-control-room.html); [Yahoo Entertainment - Kanye West 2005](https://www.yahoo.com/entertainment/kanye-west-said-george-bush-doesnt-care-about-black-people-on-this-day-in-2005-130006321.html)*

## 6. The 2008 Financial Crisis

### Origins: Predatory Lending

**Racial Targeting:**
- As the Federal Reserve lowered interest rates from 2000-2003, institutions "increasingly targeted low-income homebuyers, largely belonging to racial minorities, with high-risk loans"
- "Compared to whites with similar credit profiles, down payment ratios, personal characteristics, and residential locations, African Americans were much more likely to receive subprime loans"
- **"High-income blacks and Latinos were more likely to receive subprime loans than even poor whites"**

**Regulatory Failure:**
This predatory lending "went unattended by regulators."

*Sources: [ACLU - Impact on Racial Wealth Gap](https://www.aclu.org/wp-content/uploads/publications/discrimlend_final.pdf); [American Prospect - Black Wealth Loss](https://prospect.org/justice/staggering-loss-black-wealth-due-subprime-scandal-continues-unabated/)*

### Disproportionate Impact

**Geographic Concentration:**
- "Both subprime lending and subsequent foreclosures were more common in highly segregated metropolitan areas"
- "Segregation created easily identifiable local markets for subprime mortgage lenders and facilitated racialized predation"
- "The rise in foreclosures hit black and Hispanic neighborhoods with particular force"

**The Wealth Gap Exploded:**
- Before the recession, the racial wealth gap was decreasing and hit a "historic low point" with white wealth 6 times Black wealth
- The gap "has been increasing since around 2007 and is now the highest it has been since 1989" when white household wealth was 17 times Black household wealth
- In 2005, Black households had **9 cents** for every dollar of white wealth
- By 2009, this dropped to **5 cents**
- By 2011, it inched up only to **6 cents**

*Sources: [PMC - Racial Segregation and Foreclosure](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4193596/); [City Bar Justice Center - Foreclosure and Racial Wealth Gap](https://www.citybarjusticecenter.org/news/foreclosure-defense-helps-decrease-racial-wealth-gap/)*

### Banks Bailed Out, Homeowners Foreclosed

**The Response:**
- "Governments around the world deployed massive bailouts of financial institutions"
- The response to the foreclosure crisis affecting homeowners was characterized as **"Too Little, Too Late, and Too Timid"**
- The Obama administration's Home Affordable Modification Program (HAMP) was created to help homeowners, but "the foreclosure crisis continued to eat away at black wealth"

**Long-Term Impact:**
- "The racial homeownership gap has barely changed for 40 years"
- In 1976, 44% of African-American families owned their own home
- By 2016, that rate had **fallen by 2%**
- The foreclosure crisis "will also present new challenges to the next generation as the likelihood of receiving wealth passed down from their parents is disappearing very quickly"
- "When it comes to inherited wealth, blacks often don't receive any. Often, the home is the only substantial asset a black family has"

*Sources: [Center for American Progress - 2008 Housing Crisis](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/2008-housing-crisis/); [EPI - Great Recession and Homeownership](https://www.epi.org/blog/the-great-recession-education-race-and-homeownership/)*

## 7. The Obama Era (2009-2017)

### Drone Warfare

**Scale Comparison:**
- Obama authorized **542 drone strikes** that killed an estimated 3,797 people, including 324 civilians
- Bureau of Investigative Journalism counted **563 air strikes** in Somalia, Yemen, and Pakistan
- Bush authorized only **51 drone strikes** from 2004-2009
- **"In the year after taking office, Obama ordered more drone strikes than Bush did during his entire presidency"**
- By 2013, Obama had authorized more than 400 strikes, making drones "the centerpiece of U.S. counterterrorism strategy"

**Geographic Scope:**
In 2016, the Obama administration dropped **26,171 bombs** across seven countries: Syria, Iraq, Afghanistan, Libya, Yemen, Somalia, and Pakistan.

**Civilian Casualty Dispute:**
- The Obama administration claimed **64-116** civilians killed from 2009-2016
- The Bureau of Investigative Journalism estimated **380-801** - six times higher
- Human rights groups' estimates ranged as high as **1,100** killed

**Oversight:**
- Obama required high-level approval for strikes outside active war zones
- He personally approved every strike in Yemen and Somalia and "the more complex and risky strikes in Pakistan"
- Obama "insisted on deciding whether to approve a strike unless the CIA had a 'near certainty' that no civilian deaths would result"

*Sources: [Bureau of Investigative Journalism - Obama's Drone War](https://www.thebureauinvestigates.com/stories/2017-01-17/obamas-covert-drone-war-in-numbers-ten-times-more-strikes-than-bush); [CFR - Obama's Final Drone Strike Data](https://www.cfr.org/blog/obamas-final-drone-strike-data); [PBS - Obama Reveals Civilian Deaths](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/obama-to-disclose-how-many-civilians-died-in-u-s-drone-attacks)*

### Deportations: "Deporter in Chief"

**The Record:**
- Obama was labeled "deporter in chief" by immigrant rights advocates
- However, "a closer examination of his administration's record paints a more nuanced picture"

**Policy Shift:**
- Obama focused on removing **recent border crossers and criminals** rather than long-term residents
- Interior removals decreased sharply: **181,798** in FY 2009 to **65,332** in FY 2016
- Border removals increased: **207,525** to **279,022** over the same period
- By FY 2016, **94%** of removals occurred within Priority 1 (recent border crossers and criminals)

*Source: [Migration Policy Institute - Obama Record on Deportations](https://www.migrationpolicy.org/article/obama-record-deportations-deporter-chief-or-not)*

### Hope vs. Reality

The Obama presidency represented both historic progress (first Black president) and the limits of that progress:
- Expanded drone warfare to unprecedented levels
- Failed to close Guantanamo Bay despite campaign promises
- Deported millions despite rhetoric of immigration reform
- Achieved healthcare expansion (ACA) but not universal coverage
- Faced unprecedented Republican obstruction, including blocked Supreme Court nomination

## 8. Ferguson and Black Lives Matter (2014)

### The Shooting of Michael Brown

On August 9, 2014, police officer Darren Wilson shot and killed Michael Brown, an 18-year-old unarmed Black man, in Ferguson, Missouri.

**What Happened:**
- Wilson saw Brown and a friend walking in the street and demanded they get on the sidewalk
- An altercation occurred through Wilson's car window, during which Wilson fired twice
- Brown tried to leave; Wilson pursued on foot
- Brown turned to face Wilson, who then fired **12 shots, hitting Brown 6 times**
- Witnesses reported Brown was surrendering with hands raised; Wilson claimed Brown assaulted him

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Killing of Michael Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_of_Michael_Brown); [History.com - Michael Brown](https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/august-9/michael-brown-killed-by-police-ferguson-mo)*

### Protests and Response

**The Uprising:**
- Protests and riots erupted in Ferguson and spread nationwide
- Protesters linked Brown's death to the 2012 murder of Trayvon Martin
- **"Hands up, don't shoot"** became a rallying cry
- "Protestors burned cars and buildings and blocked streets"
- "Police responded to protestors and civilians with violence and force, deploying military vehicles, brandishing semiautomatic weapons, firing tear gas and rubber bullets into crowds, and arresting demonstrators and journalists"

**Black Lives Matter:**
- "The lack of accountability for the death of Michael Brown catalyzed the Black Lives Matter movement throughout the nation"
- "Brown's name, the phrase 'Hands up, don't shoot' and the very mention of Ferguson quickly entered the lexicon of the growing Black Lives Matter movement"

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Ferguson Unrest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferguson_unrest); [Leadership Conference - Remembering Ferguson](https://civilrights.org/blog/remembering-ferguson-10-years-later/)*

### Aftermath and Investigations

**Grand Jury and DOJ:**
- In November 2014, a grand jury declined to indict Officer Wilson
- The DOJ concluded Wilson acted in self-defense
- Wilson resigned from Ferguson police on November 29; his lawyer said he "will never be a police officer again"

**But the DOJ Also Found:**
- The Ferguson Police Department "routinely violated the civil rights of its African American residents"
- The department was "incentivized and encouraged to lobby municipal fines and fees on individuals to generate revenue for the city"
- These fines "disproportionately were issued to Black people"

**Policy Response:**
- President Obama announced $75 million for police body cameras
- 34 states passed approximately **80 bills, executive orders, or resolutions** addressing policing in 2015-2016
- According to AP, "the top news story of 2014 was police killings of unarmed black people"

*Sources: [DOJ Report on Michael Brown](https://www.justice.gov/sites/default/files/opa/press-releases/attachments/2015/03/04/doj_report_on_shooting_of_michael_brown_1.pdf); [NPR - Ferguson 10 Years Later](https://www.npr.org/2024/08/09/nx-s1-5064675/michael-brown-ferguson-killing-10-years)*

## 9. The Trump Era (2017-2021, 2025-Present)

### The Muslim Ban

**Executive Order 13769 (January 27, 2017):**
- Imposed 90-day travel restriction on seven majority-Muslim countries (Iran, Iraq, Libya, Somalia, Sudan, Syria, Yemen)
- Suspended refugee resettlement for 120 days
- Banned Syrian refugees indefinitely
- Lowered refugee cap from 110,000 to 50,000

**Origins:**
On December 7, 2015, candidate Trump called for "a total and complete shutdown of Muslims entering the United States until our country's representatives can figure out what the hell is going on."

Rudy Giuliani later said on Fox News that Trump asked him for guidance: "He called me up and said, 'Put a commission together, show me the right way to do it legally.'" (referring to a "Muslim ban")

**Legal Battles:**
- First ban blocked by courts on February 3, 2017
- Second version issued March 6, 2017
- Third version eventually upheld by Supreme Court 5-4 on June 26, 2018
- 2020 expansion added six more countries

**Impact:**
- "Families were separated, couples were forced to live apart, and communities were unable to reunite for milestones of joy and grief"
- One plaintiff was finally able to reunite with his wife and three children "after four years of painful separation"

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Executive Order 13769](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_Order_13769); [ACLU Washington - Muslim Ban Timeline](https://www.aclu-wa.org/timeline-muslim-ban/); [Immigration History - Muslim Travel Ban](https://immigrationhistory.org/item/muslim-travel-ban/)*

### Family Separation Policy

**Zero Tolerance:**
- On April 6, 2018, Attorney General Jeff Sessions signed a memo encouraging prosecution of all unauthorized border crossers
- On May 4, 2018, Secretary Nielsen required all such cases be referred for prosecution, including adults with children
- Children were separated and placed under HHS supervision while parents were prosecuted or deported

**Origins:**
- The idea came from Tom Homan, head of ICE under Trump
- He first proposed family separation as a "deterrent" under Obama
- Obama's DHS Secretary Jeh Johnson said no
- Trump approved it

**Scale:**
- Family separations actually began in summer **2017**, before the April 2018 announcement
- A "pilot program" ran in El Paso from May-October 2017
- More than **5,500 children**, including infants, were separated
- Before Trump's June 20, 2018 executive order ending the policy, over **2,800 children** had been separated

**No System for Reunification:**
- "The policy did not include measures to reunite the families it had separated"
- Scott Lloyd, director of Office of Refugee Resettlement, "directed his staff not to maintain a list of children who had been separated"
- "There was no method to track both parent and child as they moved through the system"
- Some reunifications took months; in hundreds of cases, **parents were deported without their children**

**Ongoing Separations:**
Despite the policy's official end, family separations continued for at least 18 months, with an estimated **1,100 families** separated between June 2018 and end of 2019.

**Reunification Status:**
Biden's 2021 task force identified 3,913 separated children. As of their initial report, **2,127 children had not been reunited** with their families.

*Sources: [Wikipedia - Family Separation Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trump_administration_family_separation_policy); [American Immigration Council - Family Separation](https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/report/family-separation-policy/); [NPR - How Family Separation Came to Pass](https://www.npr.org/2022/08/11/1116917364/how-the-trump-white-house-misled-the-world-about-its-family-separation-policy)*

### January 6, 2021

**The Attack:**
On January 6, 2021, a mob of Trump supporters attacked the US Capitol, disrupting the joint session of Congress certifying Biden's election victory.

**Timeline:**
- Trump concluded his rally at the Ellipse, telling the crowd: "We fight like hell. And if you don't fight like hell, you're not going to have a country anymore"
- At 1:10 p.m., Trump finished speaking and urged the crowd to march to the Capitol
- At 2:13 p.m., rioters broke through police lines; Proud Boys member Dominic Pezzola used a stolen police shield to break a window
- At 2:25 p.m., one minute after Trump tweeted attacking Pence, Secret Service evacuated Pence and his family
- Rioters came within **40 feet** of Vice President Pence
- The Capitol was cleared by mid-evening; the vote count concluded by morning of January 7

**Deaths and Injuries:**
- **5 deaths** within 36 hours: one shot by Capitol Police, one drug overdose, three natural causes including Officer Brian Sicknick (stroke linked to assault)
- **100+ Capitol Police officers** reported injured
- Two Metropolitan Police officers who responded committed suicide in the following months

**Damage:**
- Estimated **$1.5 million** in damage to the Capitol
- Pipe bombs found at DNC and RNC headquarters
- Molotov cocktails discovered in a vehicle near the Capitol

**Prosecutions:**
- Largest federal investigation in American history
- More than **1,500 arrests** from all 50 states
- Charges ranged from trespassing to seditious conspiracy
- Proud Boys leader Enrique Tarrio: **22 years**
- Oath Keepers leader Stewart Rhodes: **18 years**

**Impeachment and Acquittal:**
- House voted 232-197 to impeach Trump for incitement of insurrection (January 13, 2021)
- Senate acquitted: 57 voted guilty, 10 short of required 67

**Classification:**
"Because its object was to prevent a legitimate president-elect from assuming office, the attack was widely regarded as an insurrection or attempted coup d'etat. The FBI and other law-enforcement agencies also considered it an act of domestic terrorism."

*Sources: [Wikipedia - January 6 Capitol Attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/January_6_United_States_Capitol_attack); [Britannica - January 6 Attack](https://www.britannica.com/event/January-6-U-S-Capitol-attack); [FactCheck - Capitol Riot Deaths](https://www.factcheck.org/2021/11/how-many-died-as-a-result-of-capitol-riot/)*

## 10. COVID-19 Pandemic (2020-2023)

### Death Toll

**US Deaths:**
- From 2020-2023: **1,167,362** COVID-related deaths (8.91% of all US deaths)
- The US reported over **1.1 million deaths**, accounting for approximately **16% of global mortality** despite having only **4% of the world's population**

*Sources: [PMC - COVID-19 Mortality Disparities](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12487207/); [KFF - COVID-19 Cases and Deaths](https://www.kff.org/racial-equity-and-health-policy/issue-brief/covid-19-cases-and-deaths-by-race-ethnicity-current-data-and-changes-over-time/)*

### Racial Disparities

**Who Died:**
From 2020-2023, those with higher COVID-19 mortality included:
- Males
- Older adults
- Racial/ethnic minority groups, notably:
  - Non-Hispanic American Indian/Alaska Native (AI/AN)
  - Non-Hispanic Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander (NH/PI)
  - Non-Hispanic African American
  - Hispanic populations

**"Indigenous Americans continue to suffer the highest rates of loss."**

**Early Pandemic Disparities (2020):**
- As of July 2020, Hispanic people were **5 times more likely** to die than white people
- AIAN and Black people were roughly **4 and 3 times** as likely to die as white people
- Between 2019-2020:
  - Hispanic life expectancy dropped **4.0 years**
  - Black life expectancy dropped **3.3 years**
  - White life expectancy dropped **1.2 years**
  - Asian life expectancy dropped **2.0 years**

**The Hispanic Mortality Paradox Disrupted:**
Historically, Hispanic individuals showed lower mortality than white populations despite socioeconomic disadvantages (the "Hispanic Mortality Paradox"). During COVID, "this paradox appeared to diminish" as Hispanic individuals experienced "approximately 1.5-fold higher cumulative age-adjusted mortality rate than non-Hispanic White populations."

*Sources: [APM Research Lab - Color of Coronavirus](https://www.apmresearchlab.org/covid/deaths-by-race); [CDC - Health Disparities](https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nvss/vsrr/covid19/health_disparities.htm)*

### Factors Driving Disparities

**Exposure Risk:**
Higher infection rates among people of color "likely reflect increased exposure risk" including:
- Being more likely to work in jobs that cannot be done remotely
- Living in larger households
- Relying on public transportation

**Hispanic Community:**
- Essential employment with higher exposure
- Lower early vaccination rates
- Socioeconomic instability
- Barriers to timely healthcare access

**Age Distribution:**
"A majority of COVID-19 deaths among Hispanic/Latino, non-Hispanic Black, non-Hispanic AI/AN, and non-Hispanic NHOPI were among persons aged under 75 years" - a consistently younger age of death than white populations.

*Sources: [COVID Tracking Project - Racial Data](https://covidtracking.com/race/); [JAMA Network - Racial and Ethnic Disparities](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2818873)*
