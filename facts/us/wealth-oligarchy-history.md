## Overview

This document traces the history of wealth concentration in America from the Gilded Age to the present, examining how extreme economic inequality has translated into political power, effectively transforming American democracy into what some scholars argue is an oligarchy.

---

## Part I: Historical Wealth Concentration

### The Gilded Age (1870s-1900s)

The term "Gilded Age" was coined by Mark Twain and Charles Dudley Warner in their 1873 novel, suggesting a thin gold covering over a base of corruption and inequality.

#### The Robber Barons

The era saw unprecedented concentration of wealth among a small group of industrialists:

| Industrialist | Industry | Control |
|--------------|----------|---------|
| John D. Rockefeller | Oil (Standard Oil) | ~90% of U.S. oil refining |
| Andrew Carnegie | Steel | Dominant market position |
| J.P. Morgan | Banking/Finance | Controlled major railroads, U.S. Steel |
| Cornelius Vanderbilt | Railroads/Shipping | Railroad monopolies |
| Jay Gould | Railroads/Speculation | Financial manipulation |

**Source:** [History of United States antitrust law - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_United_States_antitrust_law)

#### The Trust System

- Companies used horizontal and vertical integration to dominate industries
- Trusts were created to circumvent state laws against monopolies
- Standard Oil controlled approximately 90% of U.S. oil refining
- Trusts eliminated competition and allowed price-fixing with impunity

> "The trusts sped up mergers and eliminated competition among their members. They also concentrated control of national wealth in the hands of a few millionaire families. As monopolies, the trusts often could dictate whatever prices and wages they wanted with little fear of competition."

**Source:** [BRIA - Progressives and the Era of Trustbusting](https://teachdemocracy.org/online-lessons/bill-of-rights-in-action/bria-23-1-b-progressives-and-the-era-of-trustbusting-4)

#### Wealth Concentration Statistics

- By 1890, the richest 1% of Americans owned more wealth than the bottom 50% combined
- A handful of families controlled entire industries
- Workers had no protections and often labored in dangerous conditions for subsistence wages

### Progressive Era Reforms (1890s-1920s)

The Progressive Era emerged as a direct response to Gilded Age excesses.

#### Key Antitrust Legislation

**Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)**
- First federal law to limit cartels and monopolies
- Made it illegal to restrain trade or form monopolies
- Gave the Justice Department power to go to federal court

**Clayton Antitrust Act (1914)**
- Prohibited specific anti-competitive practices
- Banned price discrimination and tying arrangements
- Established the Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

**Source:** [History of United States antitrust law - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_United_States_antitrust_law)

#### Trust-Busting Presidents

| President | Term | Actions |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Theodore Roosevelt | 1901-1909 | 42 antitrust lawsuits; broke up Northern Securities |
| William Howard Taft | 1909-1913 | Over 90 antitrust lawsuits |
| Woodrow Wilson | 1913-1921 | Created FTC, Clayton Act |

**Major Monopoly Breakups:**

1. **Standard Oil (1911)** - Broken into 34 separate companies including:
   - Standard Oil of New Jersey (later Exxon, now ExxonMobil)
   - Standard Oil of Indiana (Amoco)
   - Standard Oil of New York (Mobil)
   - Standard Oil of California (Chevron)

2. **American Tobacco (1911)** - Split into multiple entities

**Source:** [The Square Deal - Gilder Lehrman Institute](https://www.gilderlehrman.org/history-resources/essays/square-deal-theodore-roosevelt-and-themes-progressive-reform)

#### What Worked

- Breaking up monopolies increased competition
- Created legal framework for regulating corporate behavior
- Established precedent that extreme concentration was against public interest
- Led to lower prices and more choices for consumers

### New Deal Era Wealth Redistribution (1933-1945)

#### Key Redistributive Policies

**Wealth Tax Act of 1935 (Revenue Act of 1935)**
- Imposed income tax of 79% on incomes over $5 million
- Called a "soak the rich tax" by critics
- Top rate eventually reached 94% during WWII

**Labor Protections:**
- National Labor Relations Act (Wagner Act) - protected union organizing
- Fair Labor Standards Act - established minimum wage and overtime
- Social Security Act - created retirement safety net

**Source:** [New Deal - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Deal)

#### Impact on Inequality

> "The New Deal may have 'saved capitalism' and restored 'faith in democracy,' but it did so by changing the social balance of power, raising wages, and dismantling entrenched patterns of wealth and income inequality."

**Source:** [Completing the unfinished New Deal - Equitable Growth](https://equitablegrowth.org/completing-the-unfinished-new-deal-to-overcome-21st-century-u-s-economic-inequality/)

#### Limitations

- Excise taxes (on beer, liquor, cigarettes) fell disproportionately on middle class and poor
- Black and brown workers systematically excluded from key programs
- Social Security excluded domestic and agricultural workers (predominantly non-white)
- Roosevelt compromised with Southern Democrats, limiting racial equity

**Source:** [Rockefeller Foundation - New Deal Racial Inequality](https://www.rockefellerfoundation.org/perspective/the-new-deal-made-americas-racial-inequality-worse-we-cant-make-the-same-mistake-with-covid-19-economic-crisis/)

### Post-WWII Middle Class Expansion (1945-1973)

This period is often called the "Golden Age" of American capitalism or the "Great Compression."

#### The Great Compression

The period from WWII to 1973 saw:
- Wages and productivity growing in tandem
- Dramatic expansion of the middle class
- Narrowing of income gaps across education, skills, and regions

> "From the New Deal programs of the 1930s through the post-World War II boom and into the social programs of the 1960s, federal and state governments implemented policies specifically designed to expand access to homeownership, higher education, stable employment, and economic security."

**Source:** [How Government Incentives Helped Create The Middle Class](https://historyrise.com/how-government-incentives-helped-create-the-middle-class/)

#### Key Statistics

| Metric | 1945 | 1975 |
|--------|------|------|
| U.S. GDP | $228 billion | $1.7 trillion |
| U.S. share of world industrial output | N/A | ~35% |
| Home ownership rate | ~44% | ~65% |

**Source:** [Post-World War II economic expansion - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PostâWorld_War_II_economic_expansion)

#### Driving Factors

1. **GI Bill** - Financed education and home ownership for veterans
2. **Strong labor unions** - Membership peaked in 1950s
3. **High marginal tax rates** - Top rate 91% under Eisenhower
4. **Manufacturing boom** - Good-paying factory jobs
5. **Government investment** - Infrastructure, research, education

> "By the end of the 1950s, 87% of all American families owned at least one TV, 75% owned cars, and 60% owned their homes."

**Source:** [The Rise of the Middle Class - HIS115](https://open.baypath.edu/his115/chapter/the-rise-of-the-middle-class/)

#### Racial Exclusions

- GI Bill benefits often denied to Black veterans
- Redlining prevented Black homeownership
- Unions often excluded Black workers
- Created wealth gap that persists today

### The Reagan Revolution (1980s)

The 1980s marked a fundamental shift in American political economy.

#### Tax Policy Changes

| Year | Top Marginal Rate | Change |
|------|------------------|--------|
| 1980 | 70% | Pre-Reagan |
| 1982 | 50% | ERTA 1981 |
| 1988 | 28% | Tax Reform Act 1986 |

> "The top marginal tax rate was lowered from 70% to 28% over the course of Reagan's presidency."

**Source:** [Reaganomics - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaganomics)

#### Key Policy Changes

1. **Massive tax cuts** concentrated at upper income levels
2. **Deregulation** across industries
3. **Weakening of unions** (PATCO strike)
4. **Cuts to social programs**
5. **Shift to "supply-side" economics

#### Effects on Inequality

> "For decades leading up to 1980, all incomes from top to bottom rose at nearly the same pace. But that changed dramatically afterward. While median family income was largely stagnant, top incomes soared."

**Source:** [How four decades of tax cuts fueled inequality - Center for Public Integrity](https://publicintegrity.org/inequality-poverty-opportunity/taxes/unequal-burden/how-four-decades-of-tax-cuts-fueled-inequality/)

**Top 1% Income Share:**
- 1979: 9.0% (before transfers and taxes)
- 1986: 13.8% (peak during Reagan era)
- 1989: 12.3%

**Racial Wealth Gap:**
> "By the time Reagan left the White House, the trend toward widening racial wealth was unmistakable. By 1989, the typical white family had 17 times the wealth of the typical Black family."

**Source:** [History Explains the U.S. Racial Wealth Gap - TIME](https://time.com/6334291/racial-wealth-gap-reagan-history/)

#### Fiscal Impact

> "By the end of the eight-year Reagan presidency, revenues were an unprecedented $1.3 trillion short of federal spendingâmore than three times the deficits for the eight years before Reagan combined."

The national debt tripled from $1 trillion to $3 trillion during the Reagan years.

**Source:** [Reaganomics - ushistory.org](https://www.ushistory.org/us/59b.asp)

### 2000s-Present: Acceleration of Inequality

#### Bush Tax Cuts (2001, 2003)

- Reduced top marginal rate from 39.6% to 35%
- Cut capital gains rate from 20% to 15%
- Reduced dividend taxes
- Estate tax reductions

#### 2008 Financial Crisis

- Wall Street bailouts while homeowners lost homes
- No major executives imprisoned
- Banks consolidated, becoming "too big to fail"
- Recovery primarily benefited wealthy asset holders

#### Trump Tax Cuts (2017)

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 represented the largest wealth transfer in modern history:

**Key Provisions:**
- Corporate tax rate cut from 35% to 21%
- Top individual rate cut from 39.6% to 37%
- Pass-through deduction benefiting wealthy business owners
- Estate tax exemption doubled

**Who Benefits:**
> "In 2018, the first year the law was in effect, the top 5 percent of households received 40 percent of the individual tax cuts."

> "The extensions of the 2017 tax law provisions would drop tax burdens by an average of $81,000 for the wealthiest 1 percent, and by an average of $110 for the poorest 20 percent."

**Source:** [CBPP - The 2017 Trump Tax Law](https://www.cbpp.org/research/federal-tax/the-2017-trump-tax-law-was-skewed-to-the-rich-expensive-and-failed-to-deliver)

**Pass-Through Deduction:**
> "A study by Treasury economists found that the top 1% of Americans by income have reaped nearly 60% of the billions in tax savings created by the pass-through provision, with most of that amount going to the top 0.1%."

> "In the first year after Trump signed the legislation, just 82 ultrawealthy households collectively walked away with more than $1 billion in total savings."

**Source:** [ProPublica - Secret IRS Files](https://www.propublica.org/article/secret-irs-files-reveal-how-much-the-ultrawealthy-gained-by-shaping-trumps-big-beautiful-tax-cut)

**Billionaire Wealth Growth:**
> "The collective fortune of America's 748 billionaires topped $5 trillion in September 2023, a near record high, up an astounding $2.2 trillion (77%) since enactment of the Trump tax cuts."

## Part III: The Princeton Oligarchy Study

### Gilens & Page (2014) - Key Findings

**Study:** "Testing Theories of American Politics: Elites, Interest Groups, and Average Citizens"
**Authors:** Martin Gilens (Princeton University) and Benjamin I. Page (Northwestern University)
**Published:** Perspectives on Politics, 2014

#### Methodology

- Analyzed approximately 1,800 policy issues between 1981 and 2002
- Compared policy preferences of:
  - Average citizens
  - Economic elites (90th income percentile)
  - Organized interest groups
- Measured against actual policy outcomes

#### Core Finding

> "Economic elites and organized groups representing business interests have substantial independent impacts on U.S. government policy, while average citizens have little or no independent influence."

#### Statistical Analysis

The study found that:
- When economic elites favor a policy, it has about a 45% chance of being adopted
- When economic elites oppose a policy, it has about an 18% chance of being adopted
- The preferences of average citizens have a near-zero, statistically non-significant impact on policy

#### The "Oligarchy" Interpretation

While the study itself used the term "economic-elite domination," media coverage often characterized the findings as suggesting the U.S. is an "oligarchy" rather than a democracy.

The authors noted:
- When elite and average citizen preferences align (which happens frequently), policies do reflect majority preferences
- However, this is because elites also favor those policies, not because of citizen influence
- When elite and citizen preferences diverge, elites almost always win

#### Implications for Democracy

The study suggests that:
1. America functions as an oligarchy, not a democracy, when elite and citizen interests conflict
2. The "will of the people" has minimal independent effect on policy
3. Organized business interests systematically outweigh citizen preferences
4. The political system responds primarily to the wealthy and powerful

## Part IV: How Money Became Political Power

### Citizens United v. FEC (2010)

#### The Decision

> "The Supreme Court's 2010 ruling in Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission is a controversial decision that reversed century-old campaign finance restrictions and enabled corporations and other outside groups to spend unlimited money on elections."

**Source:** [Brennan Center - Citizens United, Explained](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/citizens-united-explained)

#### Key Effects

1. Corporations can spend unlimited money on elections
2. Created Super PACs - groups that can raise and spend unlimited amounts
3. Only restriction: cannot "coordinate" with candidates (widely evaded)
4. Dark money loopholes allow anonymous political spending

#### The Rise of Super PACs

> "Unlike traditional candidate or party committees, super PACs are allowed to raise unlimited amounts of cash from corporations and wealthy individuals. They can also spend unlimited amounts of money to benefit a party or candidate, as long as they don't coordinate with or give directly to them."

**Source:** [Brennan Center - Citizens United, Explained](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/citizens-united-explained)

---

### Dark Money Networks

#### Definition

Dark money refers to political spending by nonprofit groups that are not required to disclose their donors.

#### Scale of the Problem

> "Dark campaign spending increased from less than $5.2 million in 2006 to well over $300 million in the 2012 presidential cycle, more than $174 million in the 2014 midterms, $216 million in the 2014 midterm elections, and more than $1 billion in all 2020 federal elections."

> "More than $2.6 billion comes from unknown sources, with secretly-funded nonprofits accounting for over $1 billion of the outside spending reported to the Federal Election Commission since the 2010 cycle."

**Source:** [OpenSecrets - Dark money groups](https://www.opensecrets.org/news/2023/01/dark-money-groups-have-poured-billions-into-federal-elections-since-the-supreme-courts-2010-citizens-united-decision)

#### 2024 Election Impact

> "Outside spending exploded more than 28-fold between 2008 and 2024, rising from $144 million to more than $4.2 billion."

> "Dark money from groups that do not disclose their donors topped $1 billion."

**Source:** [Brennan Center - Fifteen Years Later, Citizens United Defined the 2024 Election](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/fifteen-years-later-citizens-united-defined-2024-election)

### The Revolving Door

#### Definition

> "The revolving door primarily denotes a situation wherein personnel move between roles as legislators or regulators in the public sector, and as employees or lobbyists of industries in the private sector."

### Capital Gains Preferences

#### Historical Treatment

> "The first preferential rate for capital gains was introduced in the Revenue Act of 1921. Under the 1921 Act, capital gains on assets held for at least two years were taxed at 12.5 percent, while ordinary income was subject to the top marginal rate of 65 percent."

**Source:** [American Bar Association - Revisiting the 100-Year-Old Debate](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/taxation/resources/tax-times/archive/revisiting-100-year-old-debate-preferential-treatment-capital-gains/)

#### Current Structure

> "The maximum federal income tax rate applicable to long-term capital gains currently sits a whopping 17 percentage points lower than the maximum rate applicable to ordinary income: 20 percent on long-term gains versus 37 percent on ordinary income."

**Source:** [Tax Policy Center - What is the effect of a lower tax rate for capital gains?](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/what-effect-lower-tax-rate-capital-gains)

#### Who Benefits

> "Nearly 95 percent of the benefit of the preferential rate goes to those making over $200,000 per year and more than three-quarters of the benefit goes to those making over $1 million per year."

> "The top 0.001% of taxpayers (those with adjusted gross income of at least $60 million) received 62% of their income from long-term capital gains and 11% from qualified dividends."

### 2017 Trump Tax Cuts Analysis

#### Cost

> "CBO estimated in 2018 that the 2017 Trump tax cut will cost $1.9 trillion over ten years."

## Summary: The Arc of American Oligarchy

### Historical Pattern

1. **Gilded Age (1870s-1900s)**: Extreme concentration, robber barons
2. **Progressive Era (1900s-1920s)**: Reforms, trust-busting, pushback
3. **New Deal (1930s-1940s)**: Wealth taxes, labor rights, regulation
4. **Golden Age (1945-1973)**: Middle class expansion, shared prosperity
5. **Reagan Revolution (1980s)**: Return to inequality, tax cuts, deregulation
6. **Neoliberal Era (1990s-2000s)**: Globalization, financialization
7. **Citizens United Era (2010-present)**: Unlimited political money
8. **Tech Oligarch Era (2020s)**: Billionaire direct political participation

### Current State (2024-2025)

- Top 1% holds 30.8% of all wealth
- Bottom 50% holds 2.5%
- CEO-to-worker pay ratio: 281:1
- Billionaires spent $2+ billion on 2024 election
- Multiple billionaires hold formal government positions
- Dark money exceeds $1 billion per cycle
- Estate tax affects only 0.08% of deaths

### The Democracy Question

The Princeton Oligarchy Study's central finding remains:

> "Economic elites and organized groups representing business interests have substantial independent impacts on U.S. government policy, while average citizens have little or no independent influence."

The concentration of wealth has reached levels not seen since the Gilded Age, but without the corresponding reform movements that challenged that era's excesses.
