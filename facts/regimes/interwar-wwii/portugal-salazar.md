# Antonio de Oliveira Salazar - Estado Novo Portugal

## Overview

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Country/Region** | Portugal (including African colonies: Angola, Mozambique, Guinea-Bissau, Cape Verde, Sao Tome and Principe) |
| **Dates** | 1933-1974 (Salazar: 1932-1968; Caetano: 1968-1974) |

## Key Figures

### Core Leadership
- **Antonio de Oliveira Salazar (1889-1970)**: Founder and dictator of Estado Novo. Economics professor from University of Coimbra who became Finance Minister in 1928 and Prime Minister from 1932 until his incapacitation in 1968.
- **Oscar Carmona (1869-1951)**: President of Portugal 1926-1951 who appointed Salazar as Finance Minister and then Prime Minister. Provided political cover while Salazar held actual power.
- **Americo Tomas (1894-1987)**: President 1958-1974 who dismissed Salazar after his stroke and appointed Caetano.
- **Marcelo Caetano (1906-1980)**: Succeeded Salazar in 1968. Lawyer and academic who helped draft the 1933 constitution. Attempted limited liberalization but maintained colonial wars. Overthrown in 1974.

### Security and Repression
- **Agostinho Lourenco**: Founded the PVDE (predecessor to PIDE) in 1933.
- **Fernando Silva Pais**: Director of PIDE/DGS 1962-1974.

### Ideological Figures
- **Francisco Nobre Guedes**: First National Secretary of Mocidade Portuguesa (1936-1940), Germanophile who oriented the youth organization toward fascist models.

## Classification

### Scholarly Debate on Fascism

The classification of the Estado Novo remains contested among historians:

**Conservative Authoritarianism View:**
- Stanley G. Payne described it as "Authoritarian Corporatism or even authoritarian corporative liberalism"
- Juan Jose Linz argued fascism "never took roots" in Portugal, and Estado Novo "differed from fascism even more profoundly than Franco's Spain"
- Tom Gallagher views Salazar as a "conservative authoritarian"

**Fascist Classification View:**
- Portuguese historians Fernando Rosas, Manuel Villaverde Cabral, Manuel de Lucena, and Manuel Loff consider Estado Novo fascist
- Scholar Enzo Collotti argued the "restriction of liberty of expression, repression of political opposition, violence against civilians by political police, and corporativism" are sufficient for fascist classification

**Hybrid Interpretations:**
- The regime used "modern institutions inspired by fascism" but favored an "organic vision of society based on traditionalist and Catholic values"
- Some scholars describe it as "fascisation" rather than fascism proper
- Salazar himself rejected fascism as "essentially Pagan and incompatible with our Christian civilisation"

### Distinguishing Features
- No charismatic leader cult (Salazar avoided public attention)
- Catholic corporatism rather than secular statism
- National Union was explicitly a "non-party" rather than a mobilizing fascist party
- Conservative/traditionalist ideology drawing from Maistre, Burke, Maurras, and papal encyclicals rather than revolutionary fascist thought
- No mass mobilization or totalitarian penetration of society

## Pre-Conditions

### First Republic Instability (1910-1926)
- 16 years of chronic political chaos
- 8 presidents and 45 ministries in 16 years
- Over 40 governments in total
- Numerous coup attempts
- Labor unrest, public violence, military intervention in politics
- Economic crisis of the 1920s
- Widespread public discontent with parliamentarianism

### The 1926 Coup
- May 28, 1926: Bloodless military coup overthrew the Republic
- Supported by nearly all political parties except Democratic Party, Communist Party, Socialist Party
- Lisbon population did not resist; leftist parties refused to defend the Republic
- Established Ditadura Militar (Military Dictatorship), transitioning to Ditadura Nacional in 1928

### Economic Crisis
- Enormous public debt
- Currency instability
- Military leaders lacked economic expertise to address fiscal problems

## Rise to Power

### Early Career
- Born April 28, 1889 in Vimieiro (Viseu District) to modest family
- Father was estate manager
- Educated at seminary at Viseu and University of Coimbra
- Graduated in law 1914; became economics professor
- Helped form Catholic Centre Party in 1921; elected to Cortes but resigned after one session

### Finance Ministry (1928-1932)
- First briefly served as Finance Minister in 1926 for only five days, resigning when military rejected his strict policies
- Appointed Finance Minister again April 26, 1928 by President Carmona
- Demanded complete control over all government expenditure as precondition
- Within one year: balanced budget, stabilized currency, produced first surplus
- Became "strong man in the cabinet" by 1930

### Assumption of Dictatorship
- July 5, 1932: Appointed Prime Minister by Carmona
- March 19, 1933: New corporatist constitution approved by plebiscite (99.5% approval, with abstentions counted as "yes")
- Constitution officially launched Estado Novo ("New State")
- First time women voted in Portugal (constitutional referendum)

## Methods of Control

### Political Structure
- **1933 Constitution**: Created "unitary, corporatist republic"
- **National Union**: Single legal party, but designed as a "non-party" facade rather than power-holding organization
- **National Assembly**: Legislature restricted to National Union members; could only initiate legislation not requiring government expenditure
- **Corporative Chamber**: Representatives of municipalities, religious, cultural, professional groups
- President nominally superior but Carmona and successors took hands-off approach

### Secret Police (PIDE)
- **PVDE (1933-1945)**: Surveillance and State Defense Police, created under direct Salazar orders
- **PIDE (1945-1969)**: International and State Defense Police
- **DGS (1969-1974)**: General Security Directorate under Caetano
- Approximately 2,126 employees and 20,000 informers
- 26,375 imprisoned 1926-1974
- Controlled directly by Salazar despite nominal Ministry of Justice jurisdiction

**Torture Methods:**
- Sleep deprivation (primary method from 1950s onward, based on CIA "Kubark" manual)
- "The Statue" - forcing prisoners to stand motionless for hours with arms extended
- Beatings
- Deprivation of food and light
- Electrical torture
- Aurora Rodrigues (MRPP militant) endured 450 hours of sleep torture in 1973

**Prison System:**
- Aljube Prison (Lisbon): Interrogation and torture center
- Caxias Prison: "Modern torture chamber" near the sea
- Peniche Fortress: Most secure political prison on mainland; famous for "segredos" (dark isolation cells)
- Tarrafal (Cape Verde): Concentration camp 1936-1954, 1961-1974

### Paramilitary Organizations
- **Portuguese Legion (Legiao Portuguesa)**: Volunteer militia founded September 1936, modeled on fascist militias. Green uniforms, Roman salute.
- **Mocidade Portuguesa (Portuguese Youth)**: Founded 1936, inspired by Hitler Youth and Opera Nazionale Balilla. Membership compulsory ages 7-14, voluntary to 25. Four age echelons: lusitos (7-10), infantes (10-14), vanguardistas (14-17), cadetes (17+). Motto: "Deus, Patria e Familia."
- **Feminine Portuguese Youth**: Founded 1937, teaching "proper mission of a woman's performance in the family and the state."

### Censorship and Propaganda
- Censorship Services Regulations adopted November 1936 (not publicly published)
- All newspapers and magazines required state permits
- Editors forced to reorganize pages to hide censorship marks
- Some driven to bankruptcy by censorship costs
- 1944: Censorship Service placed under National Information Secretariat, controlled by Salazar
- Books subject to post-publication censorship; 7,000-10,000 books examined
- Cinema used as propaganda instrument through National Propaganda Secretariat
- "As paredes tem ouvidos" (the walls have ears) - pervasive surveillance culture

### Catholic Church Relations
- **Concordat of 1940**: Treaty with Holy See, secretly negotiated
- Church obtained autonomy but Salazar prevented religious intervention in politics
- Catholics who married canonically prohibited from civil divorce
- By 1960, 91% of marriages were canonical
- Motto: "Deus, Patria e Familia" (God, Fatherland, Family)
- Catholicism never made state religion
- Some scholars argue Salazarism was not "national-Catholicism" like Franquism

### Economic System
- Corporatist economy based on Catholic social teaching (*Rerum Novarum*, *Quadragesimo Anno*)
- Industrial licensing system ("condicionamento industrial") from 1931 requiring state authorization
- Autarky and protectionism in 1930s-1950s
- Late 1950s: Industrialization in steel, shipbuilding, paper, automobiles
- 1960s opening: EFTA founding member (1960), OECD (1961), joined GATT, IMF, World Bank
- GDP growth 6.9% annually 1960-1973
- Per capita GDP: 38% of EC-12 average (1960) to 56.4% (1973)
- Economic power concentrated in "40 families" oligarchy

## Key Events & Atrocities

### Tarrafal Concentration Camp

**First Phase (1936-1954):**
- Located in Cape Verde, officially "Cape Verde Penal Colony"
- Known as "Slow Death Camp"
- First prisoners: Portuguese antifascists, communists, sailors from 1936 Naval Revolt
- 340 Portuguese political prisoners passed through; 34 died
- Notable prisoners: Bento Goncalves (PCP leader), Mario Castelhano (CGT head)
- "Frigideira" (frying pan): Torture chamber with temperatures 50-60C (120-140F)
- 30 prisoners died in the Frigideira
- Prisoners cumulatively spent 2,824 days in Frigideira

**Second Phase (1961-1974):**
- Renamed "Chao Bom Labor Camp"
- Held African independence fighters: 106 Angolans, 100 Guineans, 20 Cape Verdeans
- "Holandinha" replaced Frigideira: cell barely larger than a standing/lying person
- African prisoners treated more harshly as "colonial subjects"

### Batepa Massacre (1953)
- February 3, 1953 in Sao Tome
- Colonial authorities claimed communist revolt (later disproven by PIDE itself)
- White militias and Cape Verdian workers attacked native "forros"
- 28 suffocated in police cell
- 20 burned to death on one estate
- Electrical torture; bodies dumped at sea
- Death toll uncertain: hundreds to possibly thousands
- Now commemorated as national holiday (Dia de Martires da Liberdade)

### Wiriyamu Massacre (1972)
- December 16, 1972 in Mozambique (Tete province)
- Portuguese colonial troops and DGS (secret police) conducted "Operation Marosca"
- 385 named civilians killed in five villages
- Villages bombed, then soldiers moved in
- Methods: Herding people into huts, throwing in grenades to conserve ammunition
- Revealed by British priest Adrian Hastings in July 1973, before Caetano's London visit
- Portugal denied massacre until after 1974 revolution
- Portuguese PM Antonio Costa apologized in September 2022: "unforgivable act that dishonors Portuguese history"

### Pidjiguiti Massacre (1959)
- August 3, 1959 at Port of Bissau
- PAIGC-organized dockworkers' strike broken up by force
- At least 50 killed, hundreds wounded
- Pivotal event pushing Guinea-Bissau independence movement toward armed struggle

## Colonial Wars (Angola, Mozambique, Guinea-Bissau)

### Overview (1961-1974)
- 13-year conflict against independence movements in three African colonies
- Portugal mobilized approximately 1.4 million soldiers
- By 1974: 217,000 in Portuguese Armed Forces (up from 79,000 in 1961)
- African troops rose from 18% (1961) to over 50% (April 1974) of forces fighting in Africa

### Angolan War of Independence (1961-1974)
**Independence Movements:**
- **MPLA (Popular Movement for the Liberation of Angola)**: Founded 1956, led by Agostinho Neto, based in Luanda, supported by Soviet Union
- **FNLA (National Front for the Liberation of Angola)**: Led by Holden Roberto, northern base, ties to Mobutu's Zaire
- **UNITA (National Union for the Total Independence of Angola)**: Founded 1966 by Jonas Savimbi, split from FNLA, Ovimbundu ethnic base

**Key Events:**
- Began with uprising against forced cotton cultivation
- ~52,000 African deaths during war
- ~1,000,000 displaced persons (1962-1974)
- Alvor Agreement (January 1975) granted independence
- Devolved into civil war (1975-2002) with Cuban and South African intervention

### Mozambican War of Independence (1964-1974)
**FRELIMO:**
- Mozambique Liberation Front, founded 1962 in Tanzania
- First leader: Eduardo Mondlane
- Samora Machel became president at independence (June 25, 1975)
- Supported by Soviet Union, China, Scandinavian countries

**Key Events:**
- Began September 25, 1964 with guerrilla attacks from Tanzania
- Portugal stationed 60,000 troops
- ~1,300,000 displaced persons (1968-1974)
- Operation Gordian Knot (1970) failed to defeat FRELIMO
- Lusaka Accord (September 7, 1974) ended hostilities

### Guinea-Bissau War of Independence (1963-1974)
**PAIGC (African Party for Independence of Guinea and Cape Verde):**
- Founded September 19, 1956 by Amilcar Cabral
- Called "Portugal's Vietnam" - protracted guerrilla war with high costs
- Began January 23, 1963 with attack on Tite garrison

**Key Events:**
- Amilcar Cabral assassinated January 20, 1973 (assassination failed to stop movement)
- Unilateral independence declared September 24, 1973
- UN General Assembly voted 93-7 to recognize independence (November 1973)
- First African colony to declare independence before Carnation Revolution
- Portugal recognized independence after Algiers accord, troops withdrawn by end of 1974

### Casualties and Costs
**Portuguese Military:**
- 8,831 killed
- 15,507 maimed
- ~25,000 evacuated with disabilities

**African Casualties:**
- Estimated ~100,000 dead
- Hundreds of thousands killed, displaced, maimed

**Displaced Populations (Christian Gerlach estimates):**
- Angola: 1,000,000 (1962-1974)
- Mozambique: 1,300,000 (1968-1974)
- Guinea-Bissau: 150,000 (1968-1973)

**Aftermath:**
- Exodus of hundreds of thousands of Portuguese settlers ("retornados")
- Civil wars in Angola (until 2002) and Mozambique (until 1992) killed millions more

## Fall / End

### Salazar's Incapacitation (1968)
- August 1968: Suffered stroke after fall at home
- September 6: Emergency brain surgery
- September 16: Lapsed into coma
- September 26: President Tomas announced doctors determined no recovery possible
- September 27: Marcelo Caetano appointed Prime Minister
- Salazar reportedly never informed he had been removed; died July 27, 1970 believing he was still Prime Minister

### Caetano Era (1968-1974)
- Attempted "evolutionary continuity"
- Limited liberalization: eased censorship, permitted return of exiles (including Mario Soares), expanded voting rights (1969)
- But refused to end colonial wars
- Failed to satisfy either hardliners or reformists

### Precipitating Factors
- Colonial war exhaustion (consuming 40%+ of budget)
- 1973: Career officers alienated by government commissioning militia officers for colonial service
- February 1974: General Antonio de Spinola published "Portugal e o Futuro" arguing African wars could not be won militarily

### Carnation Revolution (April 25, 1974)
- Armed Forces Movement (Movimento das Forcas Armadas; MFA): 200-300 officers
- Nearly bloodless coup
- Signal: Radio played banned song "Grandola, Vila Morena"
- Civilians flooded streets; carnations placed in gun barrels (flower market was well-stocked as carnations were in season)
- Caetano surrendered at Carmo Barracks after negotiations with Spinola
- Only 4-5 civilians killed when PIDE fired on crowd at headquarters
- PIDE abolished the day after regime fell

## Aftermath

### Immediate Changes
- Junta of National Salvation installed Spinola as president
- Abolished censorship, paramilitary forces, secret police
- Negotiations with African independence movements began immediately
- PIDE agents arrested; operations investigated

### Decolonization
- Guinea-Bissau: Independence immediately recognized (September 1974)
- Cape Verde: Independence July 1975
- Mozambique: Independence June 25, 1975
- Sao Tome and Principe: Independence July 1975
- Angola: Independence November 11, 1975
- East Timor: Declared independence November 28, 1975 (invaded by Indonesia December 1975)

### Transitional Justice
- "Window of opportunity" for stronger reaction to past than other Southern European transitions
- Sweeping policy of purges to cleanse state and society
- Second wave of score-settling targeted economic elites
- Most punitive measures 1974-1975, before new democratic institutions established
- Late 1970s: Climate of political reconciliation prevailed
- Socialist PM Mario Soares and President Ramalho Eanes favored pacification

### Democratic Consolidation
- April 25, 1975: First free election to write new constitution
- April 1976: New constitution replaced 1933 Estado Novo constitution
- First constitutional government led by Mario Soares (PS)
- Portugal joined European Community (EU) in 1986

### Public Memory
- 2004 poll: 77% proud of how transition unfolded
- 52% considered April 25, 1974 most important event in Portuguese history
- Only 17% view dictatorship positively
- Only 14% view April 25 negatively
- 1940 Concordat re-ratified 1975, with divorce amendment, remained until 2004 replacement

### Scholarly Assessment
- Samuel Huntington: Portugal pioneered "third wave" of democratization
- Influenced democratization in Greece (June 1974), Spain (1975), eventually Eastern Europe (1989)
- Double legacy debate: Estado Novo authoritarianism AND revolutionary far-left threat of 1975

## Scholarly Debate

### Core Questions
1. Was Estado Novo fascist or conservative authoritarian?
2. How does it compare to other interwar dictatorships?
3. What explains its longevity (longest right-wing dictatorship in 20th century Europe)?

### Major Interpretative Schools

**"Conservative Authoritarianism" School:**
- Stanley G. Payne, Juan Jose Linz, Tom Gallagher
- Emphasize absence of fascist movement, mass mobilization, charismatic leadership
- Highlight Catholic/traditionalist ideological basis over revolutionary fascist elements
- Note Salazar's explicit rejection of "pagan" fascism

**"Fascism" School:**
- Fernando Rosas, Manuel Loff, Manuel Villaverde Cabral, Enzo Collotti
- Emphasize repression, political police violence, corporatism suppressing labor autonomy
- Argue structural similarities matter more than ideological rhetoric

**"Fascisation" / Hybrid School:**
- Antonio Costa Pinto and others
- Estado Novo used "fascist elements" but was not fascism *tout court*
- Traditionalism and Catholic values dominated over revolutionary fascist elements
- "Salazar reminds more of Maistre, Burke, Maurras or papal encyclicals than Mussolini or Hitler"

### Recent Developments
- Rise of far-right populist party (Chega) reactivated memory debates
- Focus shifting from democratic transition legacy to colonialism legacy
- Portuguese PM Costa's 2022 apology for Wiriyamu represents new official reckoning

## Comparisons

### [[spain-franco|Francisco Franco's Spain]]
- **Similarities**: Iberian Pact (1939); both anti-communist, anti-liberal, anti-democratic; similar mottos ("God, Fatherland, Family" vs. "God, Fatherland, King"); Catholic conservative ideology; lasted into 1970s
- **Differences**: Franco rose through civil war with 500,000+ dead; Salazar rose peacefully through technocratic expertise; Violence against dissidents more intense in Spain; Salazar avoided personality cult while Franco cultivated one; Estado Novo "differed from fascism even more profoundly than Franco's Spain" (Linz)

### [[italy-mussolini|Mussolini's Italy]]
- **Similarities**: Corporatism; PVDE modeled partly on OVRA; Mocidade Portuguesa modeled on Opera Nazionale Balilla; Both influential in 1930s authoritarian movements
- **Differences**: Salazar explicitly rejected "party-state" concept; No mass mobilization or charismatic leadership; Catholic basis vs. secular/pagan fascism; Salazar maintained neutrality in WWII while Mussolini was Axis belligerent
- Salazar sought advice from Mussolini's police but explicitly distanced himself from fascist ideology as "essentially Pagan"

### Austria (Dollfuss/Schuschnigg)
- Most commonly compared regime in scholarly literature
- Both based on Catholic social teaching (*Quadragesimo Anno*)
- Both described as "Catholic authoritarian" or "clerical fascist"
- Both suppressed democracy while avoiding full fascist mobilization
- Key difference: Austrian regime destroyed by Nazi annexation (1938); Estado Novo survived WWII
