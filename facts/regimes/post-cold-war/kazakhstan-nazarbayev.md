# Nursultan Nazarbayev's Kazakhstan (1990-2019)

## Executive Summary

Nursultan Nazarbayev ruled Kazakhstan for nearly three decades, transitioning from Soviet Communist Party official to independent nation's founding president. His regime combined oil-fueled economic growth with systematic political repression, constitutional manipulation, and pervasive personality cult. The 2019 "resignation" initially preserved his power through retained positions, but the January 2022 protests triggered a genuine power transfer to successor Kassym-Jomart Tokayev and the dismantling of the Nazarbayev system.

---

## 1. Pre-Conditions: Soviet Kazakhstan and Early Career

### Origins and Communist Party Career

Nursultan Nazarbayev was born on July 6, 1940, in Chemolgan, a village near Almaty in the Kazakh Soviet Socialist Republic. He began his working life as a teenager at the massive Temirtau steelworks, working as an iron caster in conditions so harsh he had to drink salt water to maintain his body temperature.[^britannica]

**Communist Party Rise:**
- **1962**: Joined the Communist Party of the Soviet Union while working as a steel factory worker[^wikipedia-nazarbayev]
- **1972**: Appointed Secretary of the Communist Party Committee at Karaganda Metallurgical Kombinat[^britannica]
- **1976**: Second Secretary of Karaganda Regional Party Committee
- **1979**: Full member of Kazakhstan Politburo
- **1984**: Chairman of Council of Ministers (Prime Minister) at age 43 - youngest ever in the Soviet Union[^wikipedia-nazarbayev]

Nazarbayev benefited from the *korenization* (nativization) policy under First Secretary Dinmukhamed Kunayev, which promoted ethnic Kazakhs to high governmental positions. His political views were shaped during the Khrushchev Thaw and 1965 Soviet economic reforms.[^dailysabah]

### The 1986 Jeltoqsan Protests

The **Jeltoqsan** ("December") protests of December 16-19, 1986 proved pivotal for understanding Kazakh nationalism and later informed Nazarbayev's approach to ethnic relations.

**Cause**: On December 16, 1986, Mikhail Gorbachev dismissed long-serving First Secretary Kunayev and appointed Gennady Kolbin, an ethnic Russian with no prior connection to Kazakhstan - deeply unpopular given that Kazakhs were already a demographic minority in their own republic.[^jeltoqsan-wiki]

**Events**:
- December 17: Student demonstrations grew from 200-300 to 1,000-5,000 protesters at Brezhnev Square (now Republic Square)[^eurasianet-jeltoqsan]
- December 18: Special forces from across the USSR deployed; violent clashes as police prevented crowds from reaching the square[^qalam-jeltoqsan]
- Protests spread to Shymkent, Taldykorgan, and Karaganda

**Aftermath**:
- **Deaths**: Estimates range from 10 to 170, with over 2,000 wounded[^codastory-1986]
- **Repression**: Hundreds of staff fired, students expelled, 99 people imprisoned[^rferl-jeltoqsan]
- **Historical significance**: "The first big bang that undermined Soviet federal structures... After these protests the myth of fraternal unity fell apart"[^retrospect-jeltoqsan]

> [!note] Nazarbayev's Position
> As Prime Minister under Kunayev, Nazarbayev navigated the crisis carefully. He would later memorialize Jeltoqsan as symbolizing Kazakhstan's independence struggle while never fully investigating the massacre's perpetrators.

## 2. Rise to Power (1989-1991)

### First Secretary of Communist Party (1989)

On June 22, 1989, the Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Kazakh SSR elected Nazarbayev as First Secretary with 154 of 158 votes - only the second ethnic Kazakh (after Kunayev) to hold the post.[^nazarbayev-official]

Gorbachev's appointment of Nazarbayev represented a course correction after the Kolbin debacle. However, Nazarbayev recognized that success required cultivating popular legitimacy while managing Kazakhstan's complex demographics - ethnic Kazakhs remained a minority in their own republic.[^globalsecurity]

### Presidency and Independence (1990-1991)

**Key Timeline:**
- **April 24, 1990**: Supreme Council establishes presidency; elects Nazarbayev for 6-year term[^1991-election-wiki]
- **October 16, 1991**: Supreme Council sets first popular presidential election for December 1
- **December 1, 1991**: Nazarbayev wins with **98.8%** of vote on 88% turnout - as the only candidate[^rferl-elections]
- **December 16, 1991**: Nazarbayev signs Constitutional Law on State Independence - Kazakhstan was the last Soviet republic to declare independence[^npr-resignation]
- **December 21, 1991**: Signs Alma-Ata Protocol, joining Commonwealth of Independent States

> [!important] The Last Soviet Leader
> Nazarbayev is often characterized as "both the last leader left from the Soviet era and the longest-serving head of state among post-Soviet leaders."[^dailysabah]

## 3. Methods of Control

### Constitutional Manipulation

Nazarbayev systematically restructured Kazakhstan's political system to concentrate power in the presidency.

**1995 Constitutional Crisis:**
- **March 6, 1995**: Constitutional Court ruled 1994 parliamentary elections unconstitutional[^hrw-kazakhstan]
- **Response**: Nazarbayev vetoed the ruling, suspended the Constitutional Court, dissolved parliament, and ruled by decree for the remainder of 1995[^rferl-elections]
- **April 29, 1995**: Referendum extended presidential term to 2000 - approved by **95.5%** with 91.2% turnout[^1995-referendum-wiki]
- **August 1995**: Second referendum expanded presidential powers, established bicameral parliament, replaced Constitutional Court with Constitutional Council[^princeton-1995]

The U.S. Commission on Security and Cooperation in Europe noted both referendums were "marred by irregularities."[^hrw-kazakhstan]

**Subsequent Term Extensions:**
- 1999 election brought forward to 1999 and won with 79.8%
- 2005: Re-elected with 91.2%
- 2007: Constitutional amendment removed term limits for "First President"
- 2011: Re-elected with 95.5%
- 2015: Re-elected with 97.8%

### Nur Otan Party Dominance

The ruling party evolved through strategic consolidation:

- **1999**: Otan party formed from merger of pro-presidential parties[^carnegie-nurotan]
- **2006**: Merger with Asar (Dariga Nazarbayeva's party), Civic Party, and Agrarian Party gave Otan 90% of parliamentary seats
- **July 4, 2007**: Renamed "Nur Otan" ("Light of the Fatherland"); Nazarbayev became party chairman
- **August 2007**: Nur Otan won **88.1%** of vote - highest ever - and all contested seats; no other party passed 7% threshold[^carnegie-nurotan]

Nazarbayev explicitly cited Singapore, Japan, and Malaysia as models for dominant-party systems.[^astana-times-parties] Academic analysis classifies Nur Otan as a "hegemonic party" achieving dominance within the party system.[^academia-hegemonic]

> [!info] Post-2022 Rebranding
> In March 2022, as Tokayev distanced himself from Nazarbayev, Nur Otan was renamed **Amanat** ("Ancestors' Legacy").[^rferl-nurotan-rename]

### Clan and Patronage Networks

Kazakhstan's political system operates through *zhuz* (horde) affiliations - tribal divisions dating back centuries:

**The Three Zhuz:**
- **Senior Zhuz** (35% of population): Southern/southeastern Kazakhstan - includes ruling elite
- **Middle Zhuz** (45%): Northern, central, eastern Kazakhstan
- **Junior Zhuz** (20%): Western Kazakhstan - oil-producing regions[^aljazeera-clans]

**Nazarbayev's Network:**
- Nazarbayev belongs to the **Shaprashty clan** of the Uysun Union within the Senior Zhuz[^tadviser-zhuz]
- Proceeds distributed "almost exclusively to his family, which in Kazakh terms means the Shapyrashty clan"[^eurasiamagazine-zhuz]
- Key positions in government, security services, and state enterprises controlled by clan affiliates[^eurasiareview-clans]

**Structural Tension**: Kazakhstan's oil wealth is concentrated in Junior Zhuz territory (western Kazakhstan), while political power remained with the Senior Zhuz in the southeast. This geographic mismatch fueled resentment that exploded in the 2022 protests.[^aljazeera-clans]

> [!quote] Clan Politics and the 2022 Crisis
> "Most protesters in the early days of the January 2022 rallies were blue-collar men who perceived their own financial straits as a consequence of clan inequality, when first President Nazarbayev's Shaprashty clan controlled all walks of political and economic life."[^aljazeera-clans]

## 4. Personality Cult

### The Elbasy ("Leader of the Nation") Title

**Legal Framework:**
- **July 2000**: Law "On the First President" established extensive privileges[^adilet-law]
- **2010**: Amended to grant title "Elbasy - Leader of the Nation"[^wikipedia-nazarbayev]
- **June 2017**: Phrase "Leader of the Nation" officially changed to "Elbasy"[^iiworks-elbasy]

**Privileges Under the Law** (Article 3 - "Immunity of the First President"):
- Immunity from prosecution for all actions during tenure
- Cannot be detained, arrested, interrogated, or searched
- Immunity extends to all privately owned property
- Immunity covers family members living with him[^occrp-foundations]

### Capital Renaming: Astana to Nur-Sultan

On March 20, 2019, immediately after Nazarbayev's resignation, President Tokayev proposed renaming the capital **Astana** to **Nur-Sultan** (combining Nazarbayev's first name "Nursultan" into a single word meaning "Light of the Sultan/Ruler").[^eurasianet-astana]

The proposal was approved by parliament and city council the same day. This marked the culmination of decades of personality cult construction:

- By 2021, at least five statues of Nazarbayev existed across the country (up from two in 2011)[^bluedomes-cult]
- Mandatory renaming of main streets in all cities after Nazarbayev[^allegra-astana]
- Nazarbayev's image pervasive in public spaces, media, and education[^hrf-cult]

### Dismantling the Cult (2022-2023)

Following the January 2022 protests:
- **September 2022**: Capital renamed back to **Astana**[^aljazeera-astana]
- **June 2022**: National referendum removed Nazarbayev's name from constitution, cancelled Elbasy status
- **January 10, 2023**: Constitutional Court annulled "Law on the First President"[^rferl-law-annulled]
- **January 12, 2023**: Nazarbayev stripped of honorary senator and "Leader of Nation" titles[^meduza-stripped]

## 5. Economic Model

### Oil Wealth and Foreign Investment

Kazakhstan possesses enormous hydrocarbon reserves, with almost two-thirds of production from three fields:

| Field | Recoverable Reserves | Investment | Ownership |
|-------|---------------------|------------|-----------|
| **Tengiz** | 7 billion barrels | $48 billion | Chevron 50%, ExxonMobil 25%, KazMunaiGas 20%, LukArco 5% |
| **Kashagan** | 7-9 billion barrels | $50 billion | Consortium including Shell, Eni, ExxonMobil |
| **Karachaganak** | 8 billion barrels + 1,350 bcm gas | $27 billion | Shell, Eni, Chevron, Lukoil |

**Total Foreign Investment**: Over **$370 billion** since independence[^economy-wiki]

**The Giffen Scandal**: In 2003, U.S. businessman James Giffen was arrested for allegedly paying $78 million in bribes to Nazarbayev and Prime Minister Balgimbayev for Tengiz contracts. Charges were dropped after Giffen claimed CIA authorization.[^factsanddetails-oil]

**Renegotiation Efforts**: By 2023, foreign investors took up to **98%** of profits from the three major fields. The Tokayev government has initiated renegotiations to increase Kazakhstan's share.[^carnegie-oil]

### Samruk-Kazyna Sovereign Wealth Fund

Established by presidential decree in 2008 through merger of "Samruk" and "Kazyna" funds:

- **Assets (2024)**: KZT 41.1 trillion (~$69 billion) - approximately 40% of GDP[^samruk-wiki]
- **Employment**: 300,000 jobs (5% of Kazakhstan's workforce)[^samruk-about]
- **Portfolio**: Stakes in major corporations across oil/gas, transport, chemicals, nuclear industry, mining, energy, real estate[^swfinstitute]

### Economic Inequality

Despite aggregate growth, wealth concentration remained extreme:

**Wealth Distribution:**
- **162 people** own **50%** of Kazakhstan's wealth (KPMG 2019)[^opendemocracy-inequality]
- Top 10% own nearly two-thirds of national wealth; bottom 50% own one-twentieth[^codastory-wealth]
- Top 1% receive **15%** of national income (nearly matching bottom 50%'s 16.4%)[^wid-kazakhstan]

**Poverty Reduction:**
- National poverty rate fell from 18.2% to 4.3% (2006-2018)[^worldbank-poverty]
- International poverty rate fell from 51% to 14% in same period
- Sharp regional disparities: Average wages in oil-rich Atyrau were **double** those in South Kazakhstan[^eurasianet-poverty]

> [!warning] The Resource Curse Paradox
> Oil wealth enabled poverty reduction while simultaneously entrenching elite capture and preventing democratic development. See [[#Scholarly Debates]] below.

## 6. Repression

### The Zhanaozen Massacre (December 16, 2011)

On Kazakhstan's Independence Day, police opened fire on striking oil workers and bystanders in western Kazakhstan.

**Background:**
- Eight-month strike by oil workers at Zhanaozen and Aktau demanding wage increases[^opendemocracy-zhanaozen]
- One of the largest industrial actions in Central Asian history
- Workers demanded companies comply with wage agreements[^swarthmore-strike]

**The Massacre:**
- Police fired on unarmed civilians in central Zhanaozen
- **Official death toll**: 16 killed, ~100 injured
- **Alternative estimates**: Over 70 killed, up to 800 wounded[^zhanaozen-wiki]
- Videos contradicted official claims of "self-defense," showing police "shooting to kill" fleeing civilians[^opendemocracy-zhanaozen]

**Aftermath:**
- State of emergency declared; hundreds detained and beaten[^ecologist-zhanaozen]
- 37 people sentenced, including striking workers
- No official has ever identified who ordered the shootings[^opendemocracy-decade]
- Widely considered a "turning point in modern history of Kazakhstan"[^odf-zhanaozen]

### Opposition Suppression

**Democratic Choice of Kazakhstan (DCK):**
- Founded November 2001 by officials including Mukhtar Ablyazov and Galymzhan Zhakiyanov[^odf-dck]
- Ablyazov: Former Minister of Energy (1998-99), head of BTA Bank, became regime's primary opposition target
- **March 2018**: Court declared DCK "extremist"; criminalized "positive approval" of DCK ideas[^odf-prisoners]
- European Parliament (March 2019) noted DCK's peaceful nature[^odf-dck]

**Vladimir Kozlov Case:**
- Opposition politician who represented Zhanaozen workers at European Parliament, European Commission, OSCE[^odf-kozlov]
- Arrested January 23, 2012; sentenced to 7.5 years for "inciting social discord" and "establishing criminal organization"
- National Democratic Institute compared trial to "Stalin-era political trial"[^thediplomat-kozlov]
- Released August 2016 under international pressure but remains on terrorism financing list

**Political Prisoners:**
- Open Dialogue Foundation documented 48 political prisoners as of late 2023[^odf-2023-prisoners]
- Prosecutions for social media criticism, journalism, association with banned DCK

### Media Control

**Legal Arsenal:**
- 1997: 31 channels (mostly critical outlets) lost licenses[^opendemocracy-media]
- 2006: Bill gave state "unlimited power to close independent and opposition media"[^cpj-2006]
- Punitive defamation laws, criminalized "false information," powers to block online communications[^cpj-nazarbayev]

**State Dominance:**
- Broadcast sector owned/controlled by government or Dariga Nazarbayeva[^cpj-nazarbayev]
- $150 million annual subsidies for pro-government reporting (2017)[^opendemocracy-regulations]
- Print media subjected to pressure on printing companies to refuse opposition publications

**Violence Against Journalists:**
- 2012: Lukpan Akhmedyarov shot and stabbed (survived); masterminds never identified[^cpj-nazarbayev]
- 2017: Ramazan Yesergepov (previously imprisoned for "state secrets") stabbed en route to diplomat briefing

**Press Freedom Rankings**: 157th of 180 countries (RSF 2017); "Not Free" on Freedom House internet freedom index[^rsf-kazakhstan]

## 7. The 2019 Transition

### Nazarbayev's Resignation (March 19, 2019)

In a televised address, 78-year-old Nazarbayev announced his resignation, citing the need for "a new generation of leaders."[^moscowtimes-resignation]

**Retained Powers:**
- **Chairman of Security Council** - 2017 constitutional changes had strengthened this position[^wilsoncenter]
- **Chairman of Nur Otan party**
- **Elbasy (Leader of the Nation)** - constitutional status with legal immunity
- **Life member of Constitutional Council**

### Kassym-Jomart Tokayev as Successor

Senate Chairman Tokayev assumed acting presidency per constitution on March 20, 2019.[^carnegie-resignation]

**Profile:**
- Born 1953; Moscow-educated diplomat
- Fluent in Kazakh, Russian, English, Chinese
- Former Foreign Minister and Prime Minister
- Same Senior Zhuz as Nazarbayev, but different clan (Jalaira/Kushuk vs. Shaprashty)[^aljazeera-clans]

**June 2019 Election**: Tokayev won with 70.96% - significantly lower than Nazarbayev's typical 90%+ margins, suggesting managed competition to establish independent legitimacy.

> [!question] Managed Succession or Power Sharing?
> Analysts debated whether Tokayev was genuinely empowered or merely a placeholder. The 2022 events would dramatically clarify this question.

## 8. The 2022 Unrest ("Qandy Qantar" / "Bloody January")

### Outbreak and Escalation

**January 2, 2022**: Protests began in Zhanaozen (site of 2011 massacre) after government lifted price cap on liquified petroleum gas, causing prices to double.[^2022-unrest-wiki]

**Rapid Spread:**
- Demonstrations spread to Aktau, Atyrau, and ultimately nationwide
- January 4: Violence erupted in Almaty when police used tear gas and rubber bullets[^qazinform-chronology]
- January 5: Gunfire, mayor's office burned, Almaty airport seized[^foreignpolicy-january]

**Demands Evolved**: From fuel prices to broader grievances - inequality, corruption, "Shal ket!" ("Old man out!" - referring to Nazarbayev)[^2022-unrest-wiki]

### CSTO Intervention

On January 5, Tokayev declared nationwide state of emergency and requested assistance from the Collective Security Treaty Organization - the alliance's first-ever collective intervention.[^tandfonline-csto]

- **2,500 troops** deployed from Russia, Belarus, Armenia, Tajikistan, Kyrgyzstan[^europarl-chaos]
- Forces guarded "strategic and socially significant objects" - airports, telecommunications, utilities[^nesa-crisis]
- Full withdrawal completed by January 19

### Tokayev's Consolidation of Power

**"Shoot to Kill" Order**: Tokayev declared protesters "terrorists" and "bandits," rejected international calls for dialogue, ordered security forces to shoot without warning.[^iphronline]

**Removing Nazarbayev:**
- January 5: Tokayev announced he was assuming Security Council chairmanship[^cnbc-removal]
- Publicly stated: "As head of state, and from today on, Chairman of the Security Council, I intend to act as tough as possible"[^caspiannews]

**Purging Nazarbayev Loyalists:**
- **Karim Massimov** (KNB chief, former PM, Shaprashty clan member): Arrested January 6 for treason[^thediplomat-massimov]
- **Samat Abish** (KNB deputy, Nazarbayev's nephew): Removed and arrested
- Prime Minister, parliamentary speaker, state secretary all resigned
- Nazarbayev family members arrested in criminal investigations[^freedomhouse-2024]

### Casualties and Aftermath

- **227 killed** (official); over **9,900 arrested**[^2022-unrest-wiki]
- Human rights organizations documented widespread violations during crackdown[^iphronline]
- No independent investigation of events permitted

> [!quote] Expert Assessment
> "What is obvious is that Nazarbayev left the scene... But his clan, his family's law enforcement and business empire fiercely resist as they don't want to lose their financial assets and power." - Daniil Kislov, Central Asia expert[^aljazeera-clans]

## 9. Corruption and Family Wealth

### Scale of Nazarbayev Family Assets

**OCCRP Investigation (2022)**: Nazarbayev linked to **$7.8 billion** in assets through UK-registered companies and personal foundations.[^opendemocracy-78bn]

**RFE/RL Investigation**: At least **$785 million** in European and U.S. real estate purchases by family members over 20 years in six countries.[^rferl-offshore]

### UK Property Holdings

Total Nazarbayev family UK property: **at least GBP 330 million**[^opendemocracy-london]

| Family Member | Notable UK Properties | Estimated Value |
|--------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Dariga Nazarbayeva | Two mansions + luxury apartment including "Billionaires' Row" property | GBP 80 million |
| Timur Kulibayev | 215-237 Baker Street; Sunninghill Park (purchased from Prince Andrew) | GBP 140+ million |

**Sunninghill Park Controversy**: In 2007, Kulibayev purchased Prince Andrew's mansion for **GBP 3 million over asking price** - a transaction that drew scrutiny.[^eurasianet-uk-assets]

### Timur Kulibayev

Nazarbayev's son-in-law (married to daughter Dinara) became Kazakhstan's wealthiest oligarch:

- **Forbes estimate**: $2.9 billion[^eurasianet-uk-assets] to over $10 billion[^weeklyblitz-kulibayev]
- Served as Chairman of KazTransOil and Deputy Chairman of KazMunaiGas
- **Swiss Investigation (2010)**: Money laundering probe involving Credit Suisse; shelved 2013[^odf-switzerland]
- **Financial Times (2020)**: Allegedly siphoned tens of millions from pipeline project with Chinese loans[^ecoi-kulibayev]
- **2025**: Reportedly negotiating **$1 billion payment** to Kazakhstan government to resolve investigation; no admission of wrongdoing[^icij-kulibayev]

### Dariga Nazarbayeva

Nazarbayev's eldest daughter; former Senate speaker:

- **Forbes estimate**: $595 million[^talkfinance-kulibayev]
- Wealth hidden in "incredibly complex system of offshore companies, foundations and trusts"
- UK Unexplained Wealth Orders challenged; dismissed after Nazarbayeva disclosed wealth origins in court (information never shared publicly in Kazakhstan)[^transparency-uwo]

### Asset Recovery Efforts

**2022-2024**: Kazakh investigators recovered **$1.7 billion** in "illegally appropriated assets" from Nazarbayev relatives and associates.[^carnegie-tokayev]

**June 2022**: Tokayev announced commission to "return cash illegally taken out of Kazakhstan" by "a narrow circle of people who had illegally taken over the larger portion of the country's wealth."[^icij-kulibayev]

## 10. Current Status (2023-Present)

### Nazarbayev's Diminished Position

- All official titles removed (Elbasy, Security Council chair, honorary senator)
- Law "On the First President" annulled; immunity revoked
- January 2023: Hospitalized; successful heart surgery[^wikipedia-nazarbayev]
- No longer appears at public events or makes political statements
- "No one now questions Tokayev's authority. He is firmly in control of Kazakhstan's 'power vertical'"[^thediplomat-tokayev-era]

### Family Status

| Family Member | Post-2022 Status |
|--------------|------------------|
| **Dariga Nazarbayeva** | Lost parliamentary seat (February 2022) |
| **Timur Kulibayev** | Resigned official positions; reduced public visibility; under investigation |
| **Kairat Satybaldy** (nephew) | Arrested for financial crimes |
| **Kairat Boranbayev** (former co-in-law) | Arrested for financial crimes |
| **Karim Massimov** | Sentenced for treason, abuse of power |

### Legacy Debates

The Nazarbayev era remains contested:

**Defenders argue:**
- Maintained stability during turbulent post-Soviet transition
- Achieved significant economic growth and poverty reduction
- Navigated complex ethnic demographics peacefully
- Attracted massive foreign investment

**Critics argue:**
- Systematically destroyed democratic institutions
- Enabled family capture of national wealth
- Repressed all meaningful opposition
- Left legacy of inequality, corruption, and clan politics

> [!note] Contested Memory
> Some analysts note that despite de-Nazarbayevification, "Nazarbayev and his relatives continue to wield influence" and Tokayev "is still constrained in his actions."[^voa-stalled]

## Scholarly Debates

### Competitive/Electoral Authoritarianism

Kazakhstan exemplifies what scholars term "competitive authoritarianism" or "electoral authoritarianism" - regimes maintaining formal electoral procedures without genuine competition.[^kharcenter-evolution]

**Key Features:**
- Regular elections with predetermined outcomes
- Formal opposition parties that cannot threaten power
- Constitutional structures that concentrate authority in presidency
- "Soft" repression privileging subtle control over violent suppression[^researchgate-persuasion]

Levitsky and Way's framework identifies Kazakhstan as lacking "effective competition" despite electoral procedures.[^kharcenter-evolution]

### Resource Curse

Scholarly analysis identifies Kazakhstan as exhibiting a **"soft resource curse"**:

> "A kleptocratic state used as a vehicle to extract economic rent from oil and gas exports. The relationship between resource wealth and authoritarianism is not neatly correlated, though it still has noticeable effects."[^academia-resource-curse]

**Mechanisms:**
- Oil revenues enable elite patronage without taxation (reducing accountability)
- Foreign oil company presence creates external legitimacy
- Resource wealth funds sophisticated repression apparatus
- "Great resource wealth can be a curse as well as a blessing... dominated by elite struggle for title or rents"[^chathamhouse]

**Comparative Note:** Kazakhstan is one of only six countries classified as born as "petro-states" where oil may have "fundamentally conditioned" political institutions (alongside Qatar, UAE, Bahrain, Azerbaijan, Turkmenistan).[^yale-resources]

### Personalist Rule and Succession

Kazakhstan offers a case study in authoritarian succession:

- Nazarbayev "voluntarily relinquished formal powers yet continued to be politically active"[^tandfonline-succession]
- The 2022 crisis revealed limits of managed succession when genuine transfer of power was blocked
- "Replication of elite management strategies is the direct consequence of authoritarian continuity beyond leadership change"[^tandfonline-elites]

### The January 2022 Protests

Scholars debate the protests' significance:

- Some see genuine popular uprising against corruption and inequality
- Others emphasize elite power struggle using protests as pretext
- Analysis suggests "regime-society relations" failure: inability to "reconcile neoliberal prosperity rhetoric with citizens' calls for a welfare state"[^tandfonline-january]

## Comparative Context

### Related Regimes

**[[russia-putin]]**: Key external patron; CSTO intervention in 2022 demonstrated Russian influence; similar post-Soviet authoritarian trajectory but with different succession dynamics.

**[[belarus-lukashenko]]**: Fellow post-Soviet strongman facing 2020 protests; contrasts with Nazarbayev's 2019 managed transition attempt; CSTO partner in 2022 intervention.

**[[uzbekistan-karimov]]**: Neighboring Central Asian autocrat (died 2016); similar Soviet background; more isolated economy; succession to Mirziyoyev offers comparison case.

### Distinctive Features

- **Oil wealth**: Unlike Lukashenko's Belarus, resource revenues enabled both patronage and international engagement
- **Personality cult**: Less extreme than Turkmenistan's Niyazov but more institutionalized than Putin's
- **Succession attempt**: Unique among post-Soviet strongmen in attempting managed transition while alive
- **Ethnic complexity**: Unlike ethnically homogeneous Belarus, managed Kazakh-Russian tensions
