## Key Figures
- **Samuel Kanyon Doe** - Master Sergeant who led 1980 coup; Chairman of People's Redemption Council (1980-1986); President (1986-1990); ethnic Krahn; first indigenous Liberian head of state; captured and executed by Prince Johnson's forces in September 1990
- **Thomas Quiwonkpa** (1940-1985) - Commanding General of the Armed Forces of Liberia (1980-1983); co-leader of 1980 coup; ethnic Gio/Dan; known as "the conscience of the April revolution" for opposing corruption; fell out with Doe; led failed 1985 coup attempt; captured, killed, and mutilated by Krahn soldiers
- **Charles Taylor** (b. 1948) - Former Doe government official; fled embezzlement charges; founded National Patriotic Front of Liberia (NPFL); launched 1989 invasion from Ivory Coast; became president (1997-2003); convicted of war crimes by Special Court for Sierra Leone (2012); serving 50-year sentence
- **Prince Yormie Johnson** (1952-2024) - Former AFL officer; founding member of NPFL; split to form Independent National Patriotic Front of Liberia (INPFL) in July 1990; captured, tortured, and executed Samuel Doe on video; later served as Liberian Senator (2005-2024)
- **William R. Tolbert Jr.** (1913-1980) - President of Liberia (1971-1980); True Whig Party leader; killed in the 1980 coup
- **Jackson Doe** - Opposition candidate in 1985 election; foreign observers believed he actually won; no relation to Samuel Doe

## Classification
Personalist military dictatorship characterized by:
- Military coup origins with consolidation into personal rule
- Ethnic favoritism toward Krahn group in military, government, and economy
- Rule through People's Redemption Council (1980-1986), then civilian facade (1986-1990)
- Rigged elections to legitimize continued power
- Systematic persecution of rival ethnic groups (particularly Gio and Mano after 1985)
- Heavy reliance on external patron (United States) during Cold War
- No meaningful institutional constraints on presidential authority

Scholars identify Doe's regime as paradigmatic of African "personal rule" politics, where power is exercised through patronage networks centered on the leader rather than through formal institutions.

## Pre-Conditions

### Americo-Liberian Elite Rule (1847-1980)
Liberia was founded in 1822 by the American Colonization Society (ACS) as a settlement for freed African Americans. The settlers declared independence on July 26, 1847, establishing Africa's first republic. However, the political system that emerged was deeply exclusionary:
- Approximately 12,000 African American settlers and their descendants (Americo-Liberians) dominated a population that would grow to over 2 million
- Indigenous Liberians (comprising over 90% of the population) were denied full citizenship rights
- The constitution and political system were modeled on the United States but excluded indigenous peoples from meaningful participation
- Economic and political power concentrated in coastal urban areas, particularly Monrovia

### True Whig Party Monopoly
The True Whig Party (TWP), founded in 1869, dominated Liberian politics for 133 years:
- Controlled every presidential election from 1878 to 1980
- Functioned as a one-party state despite never formally outlawing opposition
- Operated through patronage networks linking Americo-Liberian elite families
- President William V.S. Tubman (1944-1971) attempted reforms to integrate indigenous peoples but maintained fundamental power structures
- President William R. Tolbert Jr. (1971-1980) faced mounting economic problems and rising indigenous political consciousness

### Economic Grievances and the 1979 Rice Riots
By the late 1970s, discontent with Americo-Liberian rule was growing:
- Widening gap between elite and masses
- Rising cost of living, particularly food prices
- April 14, 1979: Government announced rice price increases, triggering massive protests
- Security forces killed between 40-140 protesters in the "Rice Riots"
- Tolbert called in Guinean troops to restore order, further alienating the population
- Opposition Progressive Alliance of Liberia (PAL) formed, drawing support from indigenous groups

### Military Discontent
The Armed Forces of Liberia (AFL) reflected broader societal tensions:
- Officer corps dominated by Americo-Liberians
- Indigenous soldiers relegated to lower ranks
- Pay disparities and poor conditions for enlisted soldiers
- Growing resentment among non-commissioned officers
- Small group of indigenous soldiers, including Samuel Doe, began plotting

## Rise to Power

### The April 12, 1980 Coup
On April 12, 1980, Master Sergeant Samuel Doe led seventeen enlisted soldiers in a violent coup:

**Timeline**:
- Early hours: Small group of soldiers attacked the Executive Mansion
- President Tolbert was killed in his bedroom (reportedly by Private Harrison Pennoh, later found mentally unstable)
- Twenty-six of Tolbert's supporters killed in the fighting
- Tolbert's body dumped in a mass grave with 27 other coup victims
- Doe emerged as leader of the coup plotters

**Significance**:
- First successful military coup in Liberian history
- First indigenous Liberian to hold power
- Ended 133 years of Americo-Liberian political dominance
- Doe was only 28 years old, a master sergeant with limited formal education

### The Beach Executions (April 22, 1980)
Ten days after the coup, Doe's new government staged a public spectacle of revenge:
- Thirteen cabinet ministers from the Tolbert administration were tried in a kangaroo court
- They were paraded through Monrovia in the nude
- On April 22, they were executed by firing squad on the beach near Barclay Training Center
- Executed officials included Cecil Dennis (Foreign Minister), who reportedly stared down his killers and prayed before being shot
- The executions were conducted before jubilant crowds of indigenous Liberians
- Only five Tolbert administration members survived, including Ellen Johnson Sirleaf (later Africa's first female head of state) and Vice President Bennie Dee Warner

### The People's Redemption Council (1980-1986)
Doe established the People's Redemption Council (PRC) as a military junta:
- Suspended the constitution
- Assumed rank of general (later five-star general)
- Ruled by decree
- Promised eventual return to civilian rule
- Initially welcomed by many Liberians hoping for change from Americo-Liberian dominance

## Methods of Control

### Ethnic Favoritism and Krahn Dominance
Doe systematically placed members of his Krahn ethnic group (approximately 4% of the population) in positions of power:
- Military: Krahn officers came to dominate the Armed Forces of Liberia (AFL)
- Security services: Key intelligence and security positions staffed by Krahns
- Government: Cabinet and bureaucracy increasingly Krahn-dominated
- Economy: Business opportunities and patronage directed to Krahn supporters
- By 1990, Doe's forces consisted primarily of members of his own Krahn ethnic group

This ethnic favoritism reversed rather than eliminated the exclusionary politics of Americo-Liberian rule, substituting one small ethnic minority's dominance for another's.

### Armed Forces of Liberia (AFL) as Instrument of Repression
The AFL under Doe transformed from a national army into a tool of ethnic repression:
- Purged of officers from perceived rival ethnic groups
- Used for internal repression rather than external defense
- Committed systematic human rights abuses against civilians
- After 1985 coup attempt, targeted Gio and Mano populations specifically
- Special Anti-Terrorist Unit functioned as elite death squad
- Soldiers operated with impunity, engaging in extortion, rape, and murder

### Media Suppression
The Doe government controlled information through:
- Censorship of all media critical of the regime
- Closure of newspapers that published dissent
- Interrogation, imprisonment, and killing of journalists
- Banning of political activity after 1985 coup attempt

### Political Manipulation
Doe maintained power through:
- Fraudulent 1985 elections to provide civilian legitimacy
- Selective co-optation of some opposition figures
- Use of patronage to maintain loyalty networks
- Exploitation of ethnic divisions to prevent unified opposition

## Key Events & Atrocities

### The 1985 Rigged Elections
Facing international pressure to return to civilian rule, Doe staged elections on October 15, 1985:

**The Process**:
- First elections since the 1980 coup
- Multiple candidates permitted to run
- Doe founded National Democratic Party of Liberia (NDPL)

**The Fraud**:
- Ballots taken to a secret location
- Fifty hand-picked Doe staff counted votes
- Official results gave Doe 50.9% - just enough to avoid runoff
- Foreign observers declared elections fraudulent
- Evidence pointed to Jackson Doe (Liberian Action Party) as actual winner
- Last-minute creation of polling stations at military barracks allowed soldiers and families to vote multiple times

**Aftermath**:
- Doe inaugurated as civilian president in January 1986
- International legitimacy undermined despite U.S. acquiescence
- Opposition suppressed; many leaders arrested or exiled

### The 1985 Quiwonkpa Coup Attempt (November 12, 1985)
One month after the rigged elections, Thomas Quiwonkpa attempted to overthrow Doe:

**Background**:
- Quiwonkpa had been Commanding General of the AFL and co-leader of 1980 coup
- Known as "the conscience of the April revolution" for opposing corruption
- Demoted in 1983 after falling out with Doe
- Fled to United States, settled in Baltimore, waiting for opportunity to return

**The Attempt**:
- November 12, 1985: Quiwonkpa entered Liberia from Sierra Leone with about two dozen armed men
- Briefly controlled Monrovia; captured several ministers
- Announced on radio that he had toppled Doe's government
- Liberians celebrated across the country
- However, Krahn-dominated AFL units counterattacked
- Quiwonkpa's forces lacked sufficient support; U.S. did not back the coup

**The Aftermath**:
- November 15, 1985: Quiwonkpa captured by Krahn soldiers
- Killed and mutilated; soldiers reportedly ate parts of his body (cannibalism documented in multiple accounts)
- His remains publicly exhibited on the grounds of the Executive Mansion
- One of the most gruesome events in Liberian political history

### Nimba County Massacres (1985-1990)
Following the Quiwonkpa coup attempt, Doe's forces launched systematic attacks on Gio and Mano populations:

**1985-1986 Reprisals**:
- Krahn-dominated AFL targeted Nimba County (Quiwonkpa's home region)
- Gio and Mano civilians massacred regardless of involvement in coup
- Estimated 3,000 killed in initial campaign
- Villages burned, property looted, women raped
- Raised international alarm about possible genocide

**Continuing Persecution**:
- Refugees reported ongoing harassment: "The soldiers used to say that they were sorry that they hadn't killed all the Gio people in 1985, and they were just waiting for the order"
- Economic and educational opportunities denied to Gio and Mano
- Deep ethnic hatred embedded that would fuel civil war

**1989-1990 Escalation**:
- When Charles Taylor's NPFL invaded (December 1989), AFL retaliated with massive campaign against Gio/Mano civilians
- Special Anti-Terrorist Unit and 1st Infantry Battalion brought mounted machine guns to villages
- Unarmed civilians shot indiscriminately
- Villages looted and razed
- Human Rights Watch documented systematic ethnic targeting
- NPFL ranks swelled with orphaned Gio and Mano boys seeking revenge

### St. Peter's Lutheran Church Massacre (July 29, 1990)
As civil war engulfed Monrovia, one of the worst single atrocities occurred:
- Approximately 600 civilians sheltering in St. Peter's Lutheran Church in Sinkor were killed
- Massacre carried out by approximately 30 government soldiers loyal to Doe
- Perpetrators were Krahn; victims predominantly Gio and Mano
- Demonstrated complete breakdown of state and descent into ethnic slaughter

## Fall / End

### The First Liberian Civil War Begins (December 1989)
On December 24, 1989, Charles Taylor's National Patriotic Front of Liberia (NPFL) invaded from Ivory Coast:

**Taylor's Background**:
- Had served in Doe's government as Director of General Services Administration
- Accused of embezzling nearly $1 million in 1983
- Fled to United States; jailed pending extradition
- Escaped prison (circumstances remain mysterious)
- Traveled to Libya where he received training and support
- Recruited Gio and Mano fighters eager for revenge against Doe

**NPFL Advance**:
- Initial force of approximately 150 men crossed from Ivory Coast
- Rapidly gained support from Gio and Mano populations
- By mid-1990, controlled most of country except Monrovia
- NPFL committed its own atrocities, including ethnic killings of Krahn and Mandingo civilians

### INPFL Split and Prince Johnson
In July 1990, Prince Johnson broke from Taylor's NPFL to form the Independent National Patriotic Front of Liberia (INPFL):
- Johnson was former AFL officer who had joined Taylor
- Personal and strategic disagreements led to split
- INPFL established base in Caldwell, outside Monrovia
- Three-way fight developed between Doe's AFL, Taylor's NPFL, and Johnson's INPFL

### ECOMOG Intervention (August 1990)
The Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) deployed a peacekeeping force:

**Formation**:
- August 24, 1990: ECOMOG forces arrived at Freeport of Monrovia
- Initial force of approximately 4,000 troops from Nigeria, Ghana, Guinea, Gambia, and Sierra Leone
- Mission: impose ceasefire, establish interim government, stop civilian killings

**Challenges**:
- No peace to keep; arrived into active combat
- Taylor's NPFL rejected ECOMOG presence
- Francophone states (Burkina Faso, Ivory Coast) supported Taylor
- ECOMOG became de facto combatant, fighting primarily against NPFL

### Capture and Execution of Samuel Doe (September 9, 1990)
The end came in spectacular and gruesome fashion:

**The Trap**:
- ECOMOG commander General Arnold Quainoo invited Doe to ECOMOG headquarters for a meeting
- September 9, 1990: Doe arrived during a change in guard duty (from Nigerian to Gambian contingent)
- Prince Johnson's INPFL forces suddenly arrived, heavily armed
- Overwhelmed and disarmed Doe's security; no ECOMOG resistance

**The Torture and Execution**:
- Doe captured and taken to INPFL's Caldwell base
- Brutally tortured for hours; his ear was cut off while Johnson sipped beer
- The torture and execution were videotaped
- Samuel Doe became the first world leader to be tortured and executed on camera
- His body was subsequently dismembered

**Significance**:
- Profoundly undermined ECOMOG's credibility
- Did not end the civil war (Taylor refused to accept ECOMOG-backed interim government)
- The video circulated widely, becoming infamous record of African political violence

## Aftermath

### First Liberian Civil War (1989-1997)
Doe's death did not end the conflict:
- Multiple factions continued fighting for control
- NPFL, INPFL, various ULIMO factions, and AFL remnants battled
- ECOMOG remained as combatant/peacekeeper
- Seven failed peace conferences before final settlement
- Cotonou Agreement (1993) brought UN involvement (UNOMIL)
- 1996 peace pact finally led to elections

**Human Cost**:
- Over 200,000 deaths (some estimates reach 250,000)
- Approximately one million refugees
- Two-thirds of population displaced
- Monrovia devastated
- Economic infrastructure destroyed

### Charles Taylor's Presidency (1997-2003)
Taylor won the 1997 elections with 75% of the vote:
- Campaign slogan: "He killed my ma, he killed my pa, but I will vote for him"
- Many Liberians voted from fear of renewed war if he lost
- Regime characterized by:
  - Dictatorial consolidation of power
  - Purges of military and political opponents
  - Illegal timber and diamond trade
  - Support for Revolutionary United Front (RUF) rebels in Sierra Leone
- Did little to address Liberia's poverty or ethnic divisions

### Second Liberian Civil War (1999-2003)
Opposition to Taylor sparked renewed conflict:
- Liberians United for Reconciliation and Democracy (LURD) launched attacks from Guinea (1999)
- Movement for Democracy in Liberia (MODEL) invaded from south (2003)
- By 2003, Taylor controlled only one-third of Liberia
- June 2003: UN-backed Special Court for Sierra Leone indicted Taylor for war crimes
- August 2003: Taylor resigned under siege; fled to Nigeria
- Accra Comprehensive Peace Agreement signed

**Human Cost**:
- Over 50,000 additional deaths
- Widespread use of child soldiers
- Further displacement and destruction

### Justice and Transition
After decades of conflict:
- Ellen Johnson Sirleaf elected president in 2005 (Africa's first female head of state)
- Liberia Truth and Reconciliation Commission established
- Taylor extradited to The Hague (2006)
- Convicted of 11 counts of war crimes and crimes against humanity (2012)
- Sentenced to 50 years imprisonment
- Prince Johnson never prosecuted; served as Liberian Senator until his death in November 2024
- INPFL documented for 2,588 human rights violations during the war

## Scholarly Debate

### Ethnic Conflict: Cause or Consequence?
**Primordialist View**:
- Deep-rooted ethnic hatreds between Liberian groups drove conflict
- Americo-Liberian/indigenous divide fundamental to Liberian history
- Krahn, Gio, Mano distinctions ancient and immutable

**Instrumentalist View (Dominant in Scholarship)**:
- Ethnic conflict was politically constructed and manipulated
- Doe did not inherit ethnic divisions; he actively created and exploited them
- Before Doe, indigenous groups (Krahn, Gio, Mano) had coexisted relatively peacefully
- Scholars note: "Many blame him for introducing ethnic politics into the country's governance"
- Political exclusion and targeted repression created ethnic grievances that then became self-perpetuating

### Role of Personal Rule and Neopatrimonialism
Political scientists analyze Doe's regime as example of African "personal rule":
- Power concentrated in the person of the leader rather than institutions
- Patronage networks based on ethnic and personal loyalty
- State resources distributed as personal gifts to maintain support
- Security forces as personal protection rather than national institution
- Yekutiel Gershoni's "Liberia under Samuel Doe, 1980-1985: The Politics of Personal Rule" provides foundational analysis

### Cold War Culpability
**U.S. Support for Doe**:
- Doe aligned with U.S. against Soviet Union; severed ties with USSR
- Rewarded with massive increase in foreign aid (from $20 million to $80 million annually)
- Liberia became largest per capita recipient of U.S. aid in sub-Saharan Africa (1981-1985)
- Received more U.S. assistance in 1981-1985 than in entire previous century
- 1982: Doe received state visit to Reagan White House

**Debate Points**:
- Did U.S. support enable and prolong Doe's human rights abuses?
- Would earlier U.S. pressure have prevented ethnic persecution?
- How did Cold War priorities distort U.S. policy?
- When Cold War ended, U.S. support evaporated, leaving Doe vulnerable
- Pattern similar to other Cold War clients (Mobutu in Zaire, Marcos in Philippines)

### Americo-Liberian Legacy
Scholars debate relationship between pre-1980 and post-1980 politics:
- Did Doe's regime represent genuine revolution or continuation of exclusionary politics?
- Some argue Doe simply replaced one minority domination with another
- Others see 1980 coup as genuine rupture in Liberian political development
- Question: Could Liberia have transitioned peacefully to inclusive democracy without the violence?

### Causes of Civil War
Multiple explanatory frameworks compete:
- **Ethnic grievance**: Gio/Mano response to Krahn persecution
- **Greed**: Taylor's pursuit of power and resources (diamonds, timber, rubber)
- **State collapse**: Institutional weakness made conflict possible
- **Regional dynamics**: Role of Libya (training Taylor), Ivory Coast, Burkina Faso in supporting insurgency
- **Failed democratization**: Rigged 1985 elections foreclosed peaceful change

## Confidence Assessment
**Overall**: Medium-High

**Notes**:
- **Well-documented**: 1980 coup, beach executions, 1985 elections, Quiwonkpa coup attempt, Doe's capture and execution
- **Multiple sources corroborate**: Ethnic persecution patterns, U.S. Cold War support, ECOMOG intervention
- **Death toll uncertainty**: Figures for direct regime killings (3,000+ in Nimba alone) are minimum estimates; total impact including civil wars reaches 200,000-250,000
- **Video evidence**: Doe's torture and execution was recorded, providing rare direct documentation
- **Human Rights Watch documentation**: Extensive contemporaneous reporting on Nimba massacres and civil war atrocities
- **Areas requiring caution**: Exact death tolls difficult to verify; some accounts of cannibalism after Quiwonkpa's death may be exaggerated
- **Scholarly consensus**: General agreement on interpretation of Doe's regime as personalist dictatorship that exploited and created ethnic divisions
- **Living participants**: Charles Taylor's war crimes trial (2012) produced extensive testimony and documentation; Prince Johnson remained active in Liberian politics until his death in November 2024
