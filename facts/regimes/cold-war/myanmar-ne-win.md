# Myanmar/Burma Under Ne Win

## Overview
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Country/Region** | Burma (Myanmar) |
| **Dates** | March 2, 1962 - September 18, 1988 |
| **Leader(s)** | General Ne Win (1911-2002) |
| **Type** | Military socialist / one-party totalitarian state |
| **Duration** | 26 years, 6 months |
| **Estimated Death Toll** | 10,000-15,000+ (8888 Uprising: 3,000+; 1962 Rangoon University massacre: 100+; ethnic persecution: thousands; Operation Nagamin against Rohingya: 250,000+ displaced, unknown killed) |

## Key Figures
- **Ne Win (Shu Maung)** - Army general, Chairman of Revolutionary Council (1962-1974), President (1974-1981), BSPP Chairman (1962-1988), architect of the "Burmese Way to Socialism"
- **Brigadier General Aung Gyi** - Vice-chief of staff, initially #2 in Revolutionary Council, Minister of Trade and Industry; forced to resign February 1963 over economic policy disagreements; later co-founded NLD
- **General San Yu** - Deputy Chief of Staff (1963), President (1981-1988), served as Ne Win's deputy while Ne Win retained real power as BSPP Chairman
- **Brigadier Tin Pe** - Revolutionary Council member, leftist economic ideologue; later purged for becoming too powerful
- **Colonel Aye Ko** - BSPP Secretary, BSPP stalwart, loyalist from 4th Burma Rifles
- **Sein Lwin** - Led the July 7, 1962 crackdown on students; President briefly in August 1988; known as "The Butcher of Rangoon"
- **Dr. Maung Maung** - Only civilian in BSPP leadership; Ne Win's biographer; briefly President in August-September 1988
- **Khin Nyunt** - Military Intelligence chief, rose to prominence during SLORC era
- **General Saw Maung** - Led SLORC coup on September 18, 1988; became junta chairman

## Classification
Military socialist one-party totalitarian state characterized by:
- Complete military dominance of politics and economy through the Burma Socialist Programme Party (BSPP)
- "Burmese Way to Socialism" - a hybrid ideology combining nationalism, Marxism, and Buddhism
- Extensive surveillance through Military Intelligence Service (MIS)
- Comprehensive media censorship through Press Scrutiny Board
- Complete nationalization of industry and trade
- International isolation and non-alignment
- Systematic persecution of ethnic minorities (Karen, Shan, Kachin, Rohingya, others)
- Suppression of all political dissent with deadly force
- Economic mismanagement leading to national impoverishment
- Personal rule influenced by superstition, astrology, and numerology

## Pre-Conditions

### Post-Independence Instability (1948-1962)

Burma gained independence from Britain on January 4, 1948, but immediately faced severe challenges:

**Fragile Political Foundation**:
- General Aung San, father of independence and architect of the Panglong Agreement, was assassinated six months before independence (July 19, 1947)
- Trust between Burman majority and ethnic minority leaders weakened without Aung San
- Constitutional framework created tensions: Shan and Karenni states given right to secede after 10 years; others denied this right

**Communist Insurgency**:
- Communist Party of Burma (CPB) launched armed uprising in August 1948, just eight months after independence
- Civil war spread rapidly, threatening the survival of the new state
- Burma Army mutinied in some units; only Chin, Kachin, and Karen Rifles remained loyal to U Nu government

### Ethnic Conflicts

**Karen National Union (KNU) Conflict**:
- Karen demanded independent Karen-Mon state (November 1948)
- Christmas Eve 1948: Ethnic Burman militia attacked church in Palaw, killing 80 Christian Karen, sparking wider conflict
- Karen forces attacked Insein, near Rangoon (January 31, 1949)
- Karen captured the town of Taunggyi (August 13, 1949)
- Conflict became entrenched civil war - now the world's longest-running civil conflict

**Shan State Tensions**:
- Central government troops sent to expel anti-Communist Chinese Kuomintang soldiers abused local Shan population
- Shan and Kachin university students took up arms for greater autonomy
- Calls for federal restructuring rejected by Burman-dominated government

**Failed Parliamentary Democracy**:
- Although the 1950s were nominally democratic, government failed to resolve ethnic conflicts
- Burman majority dominated government with little compromise for ethnic peoples
- Little development in ethnic regions
- Growing calls for decentralized federal structure

### Caretaker Government (1958-1960)
- Prime Minister U Nu invited Ne Win to serve as interim prime minister to restore order (1958)
- Ne Win's temporary government successfully stabilized situation
- Elections held in 1960; U Nu returned to power
- But ethnic tensions continued to simmer over federalism question

## Rise to Power

### The 1962 Coup (March 2, 1962)

**Events of the Coup**:
- At 7:00 AM on March 2, 1962, police and army secured Rangoon
- At 8:50 AM, General Ne Win announced the army had taken control due to the "deteriorating" situation
- Prime Minister U Nu, President Sao Shwe Thaik, and other leaders arrested
- A largely bloodless takeover

**Justification**:
- Ne Win claimed the coup was necessary to prevent the "disintegration of Burma"
- Cited ethnic minorities' demands for federal government as threat to national unity
- Blamed U Nu's handling of federalism negotiations
- Declared "parliamentary democracy was not suitable for Burma"

**Revolutionary Council Established**:
- 17 senior military officers under Ne Win's chairmanship
- Parliament dissolved immediately
- 1947 Constitution suspended on March 3, 1962
- Military rule imposed; country governed by martial law for first 12 years

### Consolidation of Power

**Burma Socialist Programme Party (BSPP)**:
- Founded by Ne Win immediately after the coup
- Initially an elite cadre party of approximately 50 handpicked military officers
- Declared sole legal political party in 1964
- All other parties banned

**The "Burmese Way to Socialism"**:
- Announced in April 1962 as ideological blueprint
- Hybrid doctrine combining:
  - Burmese nationalism
  - Elements of Marxist socialism
  - Buddhist philosophy
  - Military centrality
- Goal: Exclude both communists and capitalists; place army at center of state

### Power Base

The regime drew its strength from a narrow base:
- Officers primarily from Ne Win's old regiment, the 4th Burma Rifles
- The Revolutionary Council was popularly called "the Fourth Burifs Government"
- Key figures (Aung Gyi, Tin Pe, Kyaw Soe, Sein Lwin, Aye Ko) all connected to this unit
- Potential rivals systematically removed when they grew too influential

## Methods of Control

### Single-Party State (BSPP)

**Party Structure**:
- Initially elite cadre organization (1962-1971)
- Opened to mass membership in 1971
- By 1981: 1.5 million members
- Despite expansion, military dominated: more than two-thirds of top party officials were military officers
- Dr. Maung Maung was notably the only civilian in higher echelons

**Constitutional Framework**:
- 1974 Constitution established "Socialist Republic of the Union of Burma"
- Article 11 explicitly stated: "The State shall adopt a single party system. The Burma Socialist Programme Party is the single political party and it shall lead the State"
- Central Committee and Central Executive Committee (similar to Communist politburos) held real power
- Revolutionary Council transformed into single-party government, but military retained control

### Military Intelligence Service (MIS)

**Surveillance Apparatus**:
- MIS and its network of informers functioned as secret police
- Tasked with detecting and suppressing any political dissent
- Extensive infiltration of universities, workplaces, and communities
- Created atmosphere of fear and suspicion

### Press Censorship

**Destruction of Free Press**:
- Before 1962: Burma had one of the freest presses in Asia with 30 daily newspapers
- 1962: Printers and Publishers Registration Law enacted
- Press Scrutiny Board established under Ministry of Home Affairs
- Officers from Military Intelligence and police controlled the board

**Censorship Practices**:
- All books, novels, magazines, and periodicals required submission before publication
- Board could reject, cancel, delete, or delay publication without reason
- Publishers forced to cover suspicious paragraphs with silver ink
- Private newspapers nationalized or closed
- By 1988: Only 8 newspapers remained (down from 30)
- From 1965-2012: No freedom of press; all newspapers government-owned

**Repression of Journalists**:
- Burma Press Council formed to protect press freedom was quickly crushed
- Journalists arrested within months of coup
- The Nation newspaper closed; editor Law Yone arrested in 1963

### Ideological Conformity

**1975 Guidelines** from Ministry of Home and Religious Affairs prohibited:
- "Any incorrect ideas and opinions which do not accord with the times"
- "Descriptions which, though factually accurate, are unsuitable because of the time or the circumstance of their writing"

### Internal Purges

Ne Win systematically removed potential rivals:
- 1963: Brigadier General Aung Gyi forced to resign over economic policy disagreements
- 1963: Brigadier Tin Pe eventually purged for becoming too powerful
- 1977: Major purge of BSPP Central Committee; thousands purged including leftists and communist sympathizers
- Pattern: Ne Win eliminated anyone perceived as threatening his absolute power

## Key Events & Atrocities

### The Rangoon University Massacre (July 7, 1962)

**Context**:
- Ne Win abolished university self-administration and tightened campus regulations
- Students at Rangoon University protested the new restrictions

**The Crackdown**:
- Evening of July 7: Soldiers under Lieutenant-Colonel Sein Lwin violently suppressed student protests
- Approximately 5,000 students involved in demonstrations
- Official death toll: 15
- Unofficial sources: More than 100 killed; over 6,000 students arrested

**Destruction of Student Union Building**:
- In the early morning of July 8, the military dynamited the historic Rangoon University Students' Union (RUSU) building
- This building had been the symbol of anti-colonial nationalism since the 1920s
- Ne Win claimed he suspected it was a "hotbed of rebel associates"

**Ne Win's Warning**:
- Addressed the nation in a five-minute radio speech
- Concluded: "If these disturbances were made to challenge us, I have to declare that we will fight sword with sword and spear with spear"

**Aftermath**:
- All universities closed for four months
- 1964 University Education Act brought universities under strict government control
- Ushered in era of underground student activism for decades

### Ethnic Persecution

**Systematic Oppression**:
- Persecution of ethnic minorities (Karen, Kachin, Shan, Rohingya, and others) was continuous after 1962
- Ethnic Burmese monopoly of political, economic, and military power
- Federal system abolished; centralized Burman-dominated state imposed

**The "Four Cuts" Policy (1969)**:
- Military began "Pya Ley Pya" (Four Cuts) offensive
- Goal: Eliminate all contact between civilian communities and ethnic armed groups
- Cut off: Food, funds, intelligence, and recruits
- Villagers suspected of aiding guerrillas punished severely
- Scorched-earth tactics employed against ethnic communities

**Campaign Against Karen People**:
- Military launched ethnic cleansing campaign against Karen and other indigenous peoples
- Villages destroyed; populations displaced
- Created hundreds of thousands of refugees and internally displaced persons

### Persecution of Rohingya

**1978 Operation Nagamin (Dragon King)**:
- Burma Army attacked Muslim Rohingya in Arakan State
- 250,000 people fled to Bangladesh
- First major wave of Rohingya exodus

**1982 Citizenship Law**:
- Denied Rohingya access to full citizenship
- Only 135 ethnic groups deemed present in Burma before 1824 could be citizens
- Rohingya excluded from the list
- Created conditions for ongoing statelessness and persecution
- Resulted in expulsion of nearly 300,000 people during Ne Win era

### The 8888 Uprising and Massacre (1988)

**Background**:
- By 1988: Burma had become one of the world's poorest countries
- UN granted "Least Developed Country" status in December 1987
- Anger over economic mismanagement, demonetization, corruption
- Student savings wiped out by 1987 currency demonetization

**Buildup (March-August 1988)**:
- Tea shop incident sparked initial student protests in Rangoon (March 1988)
- Students from Rangoon Institute of Technology rioted
- Universities closed repeatedly
- Unrest spread throughout the country

**The 8888 Uprising (August 8, 1988)**:
- Nationwide general strike planned at eight minutes past eight on August 8, 1988
- Hundreds of thousands participated: monks, students, children, housewives, doctors, workers
- Protests spread across the entire country
- Demanded end to one-party rule and democratic reforms

**The Military Crackdown**:
- Army opened fire on protesters
- Over five days in August: More than 3,000 killed
- Soldiers fired live ammunition at peaceful demonstrators
- September 18-19, 1988: Final crackdown killed thousands more
- Total estimated death toll: 3,000-10,000 killed
- Thousands imprisoned and tortured
- 10,000 activists fled to neighboring countries

**Conditions of the Massacre**:
- Journalist Bertil Lintner: "It wasn't random shooting, as in August. More people were killed in August, possibly around 3,000, but the September massacre was carried out with military precision"
- Government official figure: approximately 350 killed (widely disputed)

**Accountability**:
- No government officials ever held accountable for the massacres

## "Burmese Way to Socialism" - Economic Disaster

### Nationalization (1962-1964)

**Enterprise Nationalization Law (1963)**:
- Nationalized all major industries on June 1, 1963
- Approximately 15,000 private firms taken over
- Included: Import-export trade, rice, banking, mining, teak, rubber
- Industrialists prohibited from establishing new factories with private capital

**State Monopolies**:
- Government created monopolies on staple goods (rice, salt)
- Farmers forced to sell rice to state at fixed prices below market rates
- Decreased farmer incentives, lowered production, created shortages

### Economic Collapse

**From Prosperity to Poverty**:
- Before 1962: Burma had one of highest living standards in Southeast Asia
- Was a leading rice exporter; second wealthiest country in region after Japan
- After coup: "Its prosperity fled along with its best and brightest"

**Rice Export Collapse**:
| Year | Rice Exports |
|------|-------------|
| 1961-62 | 1,840,000 tons |
| 1967-68 | 350,000 tons |
| 1970s | Ceased exporting rice entirely |

**Foreign Exchange Decline**:
| Year | Foreign Exchange Reserves |
|------|--------------------------|
| 1964 | $214 million |
| 1971 | $50 million |

**Growth Stagnation**:
- Per capita GDP: $159.18 (1962) to $219.20 (1987)
- Annual growth: approximately 1.3% per year - one of weakest in East Asia
- By 1987: UN declared Burma a "Least Developed Country"

**State Enterprise Failures**:
- State-run enterprises recorded deficits in 7 out of 8 years following the coup
- Centralized planning suffered from:
  - Inadequate data collection
  - Inexperienced management
  - Administrative overload
- Scholars describe result as an "unplanned socialized economy"

### The Black Market Economy

**Pervasive Informal Economy**:
- Black market represented approximately 80% of national economy during BSPP period
- Barter economies based on rice and commodities flourished
- Smuggled consumer goods in high demand
- Inadvertently strengthened criminal elements

### International Isolation

**Self-Imposed Isolation**:
- Ne Win's governance characterized by isolationism and xenophobia
- Refused to join ASEAN (formed 1967)
- Avoided entanglement in Indochina wars
- Expelled foreigners: Over 300,000 Chinese, Indian, and Anglo-Burmese left Burma by late 1960s

**Comparison to North Korea**:
- Scholars noted: "Only Kim Il-Sung's Democratic People's Republic of Korea can rival Ne Win's Burma in its determination to isolate itself from what is happening elsewhere"

### Demonetization Disasters

Burma experienced three currency demonetizations under Ne Win:

**1964 Demonetization**:
- 50 and 100 kyat notes demonetized
- Ostensibly to combat black marketeering
- Limited compensation offered

**1985 Demonetization**:
- November 3, 1985: 20, 50, and 100 kyat notes declared invalid without warning
- Stated reason: Combat black market activity
- New bizarre denominations introduced: 25, 35, and 75 kyat notes
- 75 kyat denomination chosen to commemorate Ne Win's 75th birthday

**1987 Demonetization (The Final Straw)**:
- September 5, 1987: 25, 35, and 75 kyat notes demonetized without warning
- Only 1, 5, and 10 kyat bills remained valid
- 60-80% of circulated legal tender wiped out overnight
- No exchange for old currency permitted
- Millions of citizens had their savings eliminated instantly

**Impact**:
- Students particularly angry - tuition savings destroyed
- Argument that demonetization hurt insurgents proved false: They simply used Thai and Chinese currencies
- Insurgent economies based on narcotics and border trading unaffected
- National debt: $3.5-4.9 billion (about three-fourths of GDP)
- Currency reserves: Only $20-35 million
- Directly triggered student protests leading to 8888 Uprising

### Ne Win's Numerology and Economic Policy

**The Number Nine Obsession**:
- Ne Win believed nine was his lucky/auspicious number
- Astrologers advised him that nine would ensure he lived to age 90
- Currency denominations chosen for numerological significance:
  - 45 and 90 kyat notes (divisible by 9, digits add to 9)
- Economic policy influenced by superstition rather than rational planning

**Other Numerological Decisions**:
- SLORC military takeover announced September 18 (1+8=9)
- Name change to "Myanmar" announced May 27 (2+7=9)
- 1970: Cars ordered to switch from driving on left to right after astrologer warned country had "turned too far to the left"

## Fall/End of Regime

### Ne Win's Resignation (July 23, 1988)

**Context**:
- Nationwide protests growing despite violent crackdowns
- Economic crisis reached breaking point
- Ne Win's credibility destroyed by policy failures

**Resignation Speech**:
- At BSPP Party Congress, Ne Win announced resignation as party chairman
- Issued ominous warning: "If the army shoots it has no tradition of shooting into the air. It would shoot straight to hit"
- Proposed multi-party referendum (rejected by Congress)

**Transitional Chaos**:
| Leader | Period | Duration |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Sein Lwin | July 27 - August 12, 1988 | 18 days |
| Dr. Maung Maung | August 19 - September 18, 1988 | 30 days |

- Sein Lwin ("Butcher of Rangoon") forced to resign after continued protests
- Dr. Maung Maung - only non-military civilian to lead BSPP

### The SLORC Coup (September 18, 1988)

**Events**:
- Ne Win summoned senior military and government officials to his home
- Asked them: "Can you take care of the country?"
- General Saw Maung led military takeover
- State Law and Order Restoration Council (SLORC) established
- 1974 Constitution repealed

**SLORC Leadership**:
- General Saw Maung - Chairman
- Lieutenant-General Than Shwe - Member (later paramount leader)
- Brigadier General Khin Nyunt - Military Intelligence chief

**Immediate Violence**:
- Troops went through cities firing indiscriminately at protesters
- Within first week: estimated 1,000 students, monks, and schoolchildren killed
- By end of 1988: estimated 10,000 total killed (protesters and soldiers)
- Massacres carried out "with military precision"

### Ne Win's Continued Influence

Despite formal resignation:
- Widely believed to have orchestrated the SLORC coup from behind the scenes
- Remained shadowy influential figure for approximately ten years
- Some scholars believe he maintained real power into the 1990s

## Aftermath

### Continued Military Rule

**SLORC Period (1988-1997)**:
- Military junta imposed "more draconian measures than Ne Win had imposed"
- Martial law continued
- 6,000 NLD supporters detained by 1989
- 10,000 activists fled to border areas controlled by ethnic insurgents

**1990 Elections**:
- SLORC held national parliamentary elections May 27, 1990
- Aung San Suu Kyi's National League for Democracy (NLD) won landslide victory
- NLD won 392 of 485 seats (81%)
- Aung San Suu Kyi was under house arrest at the time
- Military refused to transfer power to elected government

**SPDC Period (1997-2011)**:
- 1992: General Than Shwe became junta leader
- 1997: SLORC renamed State Peace and Development Council (SPDC)
- No change in policy of "autocratic control and repression"
- Aung San Suu Kyi spent 15 of 21 years under house arrest (1989-2010)

### Quasi-Civilian Government (2011-2021)

- Military ceded power to quasi-civilian government under 2008 Constitution
- Constitution guaranteed military 25% of parliamentary seats
- Military retained control of key ministries
- NLD won 2015 elections; Aung San Suu Kyi became State Counsellor

### 2021 Military Coup

- February 1, 2021: Military again seized power, detained Aung San Suu Kyi
- Pattern established by Ne Win continued: military refuses to accept civilian democratic rule
- Civil war and armed resistance ongoing

### Ne Win's Final Years and Death

**House Arrest (2002)**:
- March 2002: Three grandsons (Aye Ne Win, Kyaw Ne Win, Zwe Ne Win) and son-in-law (Aye Zaw Win) arrested
- Accused of plotting coup to restore Ne Win to power
- Convicted and sentenced to death (later commuted)
- Ne Win placed under house arrest without formal charges
- Some diplomats doubted coup plot; saw it as move against Ne Win family influence

**Death (December 5, 2002)**:
- Ne Win died under house arrest at age 91
- Death unannounced by government or state media
- Only a paid obituary in some newspapers
- No state funeral; only approximately 30 people attended hastily arranged funeral
- Daughter Sandar Win temporarily released to attend
- Ashes scattered in Hlaing River

**Legacy Assessment**:
- When Ne Win took power, Myanmar exported 2 million tons of rice yearly
- By 1987, reduced to "Least Developed Country" status
- Ne Win grandsons later admitted: "It was Ne Win who put Than Shwe in power, and by so doing planted the seeds of his own - and the country's - ruin"

## Scholarly Debate

### Historiographical Perspectives

**Two Main Scholarly Camps**:
1. **"Didn't Work" School**: Policies simply failed and are therefore uninteresting
2. **"Necessary" School**: Policies were required given chaos of previous regime

**Robert Taylor's Biography**:
- Most comprehensive scholarly treatment of Ne Win
- Describes him as "the most enigmatic and controversial of the first generation of post-independence Southeast Asian leaders"
- Significant contribution to historiography of Myanmar and Cold War in Asia

### Debates on Regime Classification

**Military Socialist vs. Pure Military Dictatorship**:
- Regime combined military rule with socialist ideology
- Unlike communist regimes: Buddhist elements incorporated
- Unlike pure military regimes: Comprehensive ideological framework attempted

**Comparison with Suharto's Indonesia**:
- Scholars have compared Ne Win's Burma with Suharto's Indonesia
- Both: Military takeover, long rule, economic mismanagement, ethnic persecution
- Key difference: Indonesia eventually transitioned to democracy; Myanmar did not

### Ethnic Conflict Analysis

**Scholarly Consensus**:
- Burma's ethnic conflict is "not per se ethnic... but a conflict rooted in politics"
- Ne Win's refusal of federal system intensified conflicts
- Burmanization policies created and reinforced ethnic divides

**Recent Scholarship (2024)**:
- Focus on how Burmanization policies explain current ethnic conflicts
- Legacy of military centrality continues to shape Myanmar

### Economic Policy Assessment

**Universal Agreement on Failure**:
- "Burmese Way to Socialism" described as "abject failure"
- Turned "one of the most prosperous countries in Asia into one of the world's poorest"
- Described as "disastrous"

**Causes of Failure**:
- Ideological rigidity
- Superstition-influenced decision-making
- Isolation from global economy
- Incompetent management
- Prioritizing political control over economic rationality

### Questions of Accountability

**Unresolved Issues**:
- No officials held accountable for 8888 massacre
- No transitional justice process
- Military impunity continues to present day
- Pattern of violence established under Ne Win repeated in 2021 coup response

### Key Uncertainties
- Exact death tolls from all periods remain contested
- Extent of Ne Win's behind-the-scenes influence after 1988 unclear
- Some superstition stories may be embellished or apocryphal
- Full extent of ethnic persecution deaths unknown due to ongoing conflict and inaccessible regions
- Whether 2002 "coup plot" by family members was genuine or pretext for eliminating Ne Win's influence

### Research Gaps
- Limited primary source access due to Myanmar's restricted archives
- Survivor testimonies from ethnic regions need more systematic collection
- Economic data from informal/black market economy is estimated
- Ne Win's personal papers and communications largely inaccessible
