# East Germany (German Democratic Republic) - Ulbricht and Honecker Era

## Overview

| Field | Value |

## Key Figures

### Walter Ulbricht (1893-1973)

- **Role**: First Secretary of the SED (1950-1971); Chairman of the Council of State (1960-1973)
- **Background**: Born in Leipzig; cabinetmaker by trade. Joined Social Democratic Party (1912), then Communist Party of Germany (KPD) after WWI. Rose through party ranks, becoming Reichstag member (1928) and Berlin party leader (1929).
- **Exile Years**: Fled Germany after Hitler's rise (1933). Spent years in Paris, Moscow, and Spain during Civil War as KPD/Comintern agent.
- **Return**: Arrived in Germany April 30, 1945 to organize Soviet occupation zone administration.
- **Key Actions**: Orchestrated forced merger of KPD and SPD into SED (1946); suppressed 1953 uprising with Soviet help; demanded and oversaw construction of Berlin Wall (1961).
- **Style**: Described as "fanatical communist" who modeled himself on Lenin and Stalin. Wore Lenin-style beard. Known as perhaps the most unpopular Communist leader of his era due to inflexible policies.
- **End**: Forced to resign as First Secretary (May 1971) when Soviet Union opened relations with West Germany. Retained ceremonial head of state position until death.

### Erich Honecker (1912-1994)

- **Role**: First/General Secretary of SED (1971-1989); Chairman of National Defence Council (1971-1989); Chairman of State Council (1976-1989)
- **Background**: Born in Neunkirchen to communist miner family. Joined Communist Youth (1926), full KPD member (1929).
- **Nazi Era**: Organized illegal communist youth activities. Arrested by Gestapo (1935), sentenced to 10 years hard labor for "preparing treason." Liberated by Red Army (1945).
- **Post-War Rise**: Led Free German Youth (FDJ) 1946-1955. Joined Central Committee (1945), Politburo candidate member (1950), full member (1958).
- **Berlin Wall**: Personally put in charge of building the Berlin Wall (1961).
- **Leadership**: Under Honecker, East Germany was repressive but economically prosperous by Eastern Bloc standards. Developed working relationship with West Germany while maintaining surveillance state.
- **Fall**: Refused reforms despite perestroika and glasnost. Forced to resign by SED Politburo (October 18, 1989) as protests mounted.
- **Aftermath**: Stood trial for election manipulation and Wall deaths after reunification. Released due to ill health (1993), died in Chile (1994).

### Erich Mielke (1907-2000)

- **Role**: Minister for State Security (1957-1989), head of the Stasi for over 30 years
- **Background**: Born Berlin to working-class family. Joined KPD young.
- **1931 Murders**: As communist militant, involved in killing two Berlin police officers (Paul Anlauf and Franz Lenck). Fled to Soviet Union to escape prosecution.
- **Soviet Years/Spanish Civil War**: Trained in USSR, fought with International Brigades in Spain.
- **Post-War**: Returned to Soviet-occupied Germany, helped establish Stasi (1950).
- **Stasi Leadership**: Built the Stasi into one of the most effective and repressive secret police organizations in the Eastern Bloc.
- **Infamous Speech**: Final speech to East German parliament: "Ich liebe euch doch alle" ("But I love you all") - became infamous symbol of regime's detachment from reality.
- **Trial and Death**: Arrested July 26, 1990. Convicted (1993) for the 1931 police murders, not Wall deaths. Died in Berlin nursing home (2000).

### Other Significant Figures

- **Wilhelm Pieck (1876-1960)**: First and only President of the GDR (1949-1960). Co-chairman of SED with Otto Grotewohl at founding. Former KPD leader.

- **Otto Grotewohl (1894-1964)**: Prime Minister (1949-1964). Former SPD leader in Soviet zone who oversaw merger into SED. Co-chairman at founding.

- **Markus Wolf (1923-2006)**: Head of the Main Directorate for Reconnaissance (HVA), the foreign intelligence arm of the Stasi, for 34 years. Known as "The Man Without a Face" for avoiding Western photography. Considered one of most successful spymasters of Cold War.

- **Egon Krenz (b. 1937)**: Succeeded Honecker as General Secretary (October-December 1989). Last leader of GDR. Presided over fall of Wall. Convicted (1997) for Wall deaths, served partial sentence.

## Classification

### Primary Classification
**Soviet-style Marxist-Leninist One-Party State**

### Defining Characteristics

1. **Soviet satellite**: Established in Soviet occupation zone, remained under significant Soviet influence throughout existence
2. **One-party rule**: SED held constitutional monopoly on power; other parties existed as "bloc parties" under SED control
3. **Command economy**: Centrally planned economy following Soviet model
4. **Mass surveillance**: Stasi represented most extensive domestic surveillance apparatus relative to population in history
5. **Closed borders**: Berlin Wall and inner-German border physically prevented emigration
6. **Ideological control**: State control over media, education, culture
7. **Political repression**: Systematic imprisonment of dissidents and would-be emigrants

---

## Pre-Conditions

### World War II and Its Aftermath

- **Nazi Germany's Defeat**: Germany surrendered unconditionally (May 8, 1945)
- **Allied Occupation**: Germany divided into four occupation zones (American, British, French, Soviet)
- **Berlin Division**: Former capital similarly divided into four sectors despite being deep within Soviet zone

### Soviet Occupation Zone (1945-1949)

- **Soviet Administration**: Soviet Military Administration in Germany (SMAD) governed occupation zone
- **Early Repression**: Soviet internment camps held political prisoners; approximately 65,000 died in camps between 1945-1950
- **Soviet Courts**: 756 people executed by Soviet army courts for anti-communist activities in immediate post-war years
- **Land Reform**: Expropriation of large landholders (Junkers)
- **Nationalization**: Seizure of major industries and enterprises

### Division of Germany

- **Growing Cold War**: East-West tensions made unified Germany impossible
- **Western Integration**: Three western zones merged economically (1948), then politically as Federal Republic of Germany (May 23, 1949)
- **Soviet Response**: German Democratic Republic proclaimed (October 7, 1949) in Soviet zone
- **Berlin Blockade (1948-1949)**: Soviet attempt to force Western powers from Berlin; countered by Allied airlift

---

## Rise of Regime

### Formation of the SED (April 21, 1946)

- **Forced Merger**: Communist Party of Germany (KPD) and Social Democratic Party (SPD) merged in Soviet zone
- **Soviet Pressure**: While officially portrayed as voluntary, evidence shows Soviet occupation authorities applied great pressure on SPD's eastern branch
- **Leadership**: Wilhelm Pieck (former KPD) and Otto Grotewohl (former SPD) named co-chairmen; Walter Ulbricht named Secretary-General
- **Initial Membership**: Approximately 1.3 million (600,000 former KPD, 680,000 former SPD)
- **Rapid Communist Dominance**: By 1950, over 62% of Central Committee members were former KPD. Social Democrats systematically marginalized and purged
- **Western Rejection**: Merger rejected in three western occupation zones where both parties remained independent

### Establishment of the GDR (October 7, 1949)

- **Proclamation**: SED established German Democratic Republic in response to founding of West Germany
- **Constitutional Framework**: Nominally democratic constitution but SED guaranteed leading role
- **Bloc Parties**: Other parties (CDU, LDPD, NDPD, DBD) permitted but controlled by SED through "National Front"
- **Soviet Model**: State structure, economy, and society organized along Soviet lines

### Ulbricht's Consolidation of Power

- **First Secretary (1950)**: Ulbricht became effective leader of party and state
- **Stalinist Methods**: Purges of potential rivals, show trials, cult of personality
- **Economic Restructuring**: Nationalization of remaining private enterprises, collectivization of agriculture
- **Alignment with Moscow**: Close adherence to Soviet foreign policy and domestic model

## Methods of Control

### The Stasi (Ministry for State Security)

**Scale:**
- By 1989: 91,015 full-time employees
- 173,081-180,000 unofficial collaborators (informants)
- Files maintained on approximately 5.6 million East German citizens (one-third of population)
- Archives totaling 111 kilometers (69 miles) of files

**Surveillance Ratios:**
- One secret policeman per 166 East Germans (compared to one Gestapo agent per 2,000 in Nazi Germany)
- Including informants: one agent per 6.5 people
- Nazi hunter Simon Wiesenthal called the Stasi "even more oppressive than the Gestapo"
- Some estimates suggest up to 2 million occasional informants over the regime's existence

**Methods:**
- Mass surveillance of correspondence, telephone calls, daily activities
- Informant recruitment from all layers of society (though concentrated among SED members, nomenklatura, military/police)
- Workplace informants in all major industrial plants
- Building informants reporting on residents
- Psychological harassment ("Zersetzung" - decomposition): systematic destruction of targets' careers, relationships, self-confidence
- Imprisonment of dissidents
- Infiltration of churches, opposition groups, cultural institutions

**Foreign Intelligence (HVA):**
- Under Markus Wolf, penetrated West German government at highest levels
- Most famous success: Gunter Guillaume, aide to West German Chancellor Willy Brandt

### The Berlin Wall and Border System

**Construction (August 13, 1961):**
- Built overnight to stem massive emigration to West
- Approximately 3.5 million East Germans had fled between 1945-1961 (20% of population)
- Honecker personally directed construction

**Physical Barriers:**
- Concrete wall up to 3.6 meters high in Berlin
- 155 kilometers around West Berlin
- Inner-German border: 1,393 kilometers with multiple fence lines, watchtowers, minefields, vehicle barriers
- "Death strip" with clear firing lines
- Trip wires, guard dogs, automatic firing devices

**Shoot-to-Kill Orders:**
- Border guards instructed to prevent escapes "by any means necessary"
- Order to shoot not officially lifted until April 1989
- Guards who allowed escapes faced prosecution

### Prison System

**Scale:**
- 1953: Over 250 remand prisons, penal institutions, labor camps, prison hospitals
- By 1989: Approximately 80 institutions remained
- 48,000+ simultaneous prisoners at 1973 peak
- 200,000-250,000 politically imprisoned over regime's existence

**Political Prisoners:**
- By end of 1961: Nearly 30% of all prisoners classified as political
- 1970s-1980s: 3,000-5,000 sentenced annually under political paragraphs
- Main "crimes": attempting to flee, "anti-state agitation," associating with West

**Forced Labor:**
- Up to 30,000 inmates, including political prisoners, forced to work
- Over 6,000 Western firms (including Ikea, Aldi, Siemens) profited from GDR prison labor

**Trading of Political Prisoners (Haftlingsfreikauf):**
- West Germany began paying for release of political prisoners (1963)
- Total expenditure: 3.4 billion Deutschmarks
- 33,755 political prisoners and approximately 250,000 relatives transferred to West Germany (1964-1989)

### Information Control

- State monopoly on media; all outlets controlled by SED
- Censorship of literature, film, art, music
- Jamming of Western radio broadcasts
- Television: Many East Germans could receive West German broadcasts, creating "electronic hole in the Wall"
- Travel restrictions: Visits to West only for retirees or in exceptional circumstances

## Key Events

### The June 1953 Uprising

**Causes:**
- Stalin's death (March 1953) created uncertainty
- Government increased work quotas by 10% without wage increases
- General economic hardship and political repression

**Events:**
- June 16, 1953: Construction workers in East Berlin went on strike
- June 17: Protests spread to over 700 cities and towns across GDR
- Approximately 1 million people participated
- Demands escalated from economic grievances to calls for free elections and reunification

**Suppression:**
- GDR government unable to contain uprising
- Soviet occupation forces intervened with tanks and troops
- Martial law declared
- Uprising crushed within days

**Aftermath:**
- At least 55 killed (some estimates higher)
- Thousands arrested, many received long sentences
- June 17 commemorated as "Day of German Unity" in West Germany until 1990
- First major popular uprising against Soviet-bloc government during Cold War

### Construction of the Berlin Wall (August 13, 1961)

**Background:**
- Mass emigration ("brain drain"): 3.5 million fled to West (1945-1961)
- 1,000+ leaving daily by summer 1961
- Economic and political crisis for GDR

**Construction:**
- Built overnight beginning August 13, 1961
- Initially barbed wire barriers, later concrete wall
- Families, friends, colleagues separated without warning
- West Berlin entirely encircled

**International Impact:**
- Major Cold War crisis
- Kennedy response: "Ich bin ein Berliner" speech (1963)
- Became defining symbol of Cold War division

### The 1989 Peaceful Revolution

**Context:**
- Gorbachev's reforms (perestroika, glasnost) in USSR
- Revolutions sweeping Eastern Europe (Poland, Hungary)
- Honecker refused meaningful reforms

**Exodus and Protests:**
- September 1989: 13,000+ escaped to West through Hungary
- Monday Demonstrations began Leipzig, September 4, 1989
- Originated from peace prayer meetings at Nikolaikirche (St. Nicholas Church) dating back to 1982

**The Decisive Day: October 9, 1989:**
- State amassed 8,000 police and armed military units
- Fears of Tiananmen-style massacre
- 70,000 demonstrators faced armed forces
- Chanting "Wir sind das Volk" ("We are the people")
- Security forces did not fire; revolution continued

**Rapid Escalation:**
- October 16: 120,000 demonstrators in Leipzig
- October 18: Honecker forced to resign, replaced by Egon Krenz
- October 23: 320,000 in Leipzig
- November 4: Approximately 1 million gathered at Alexanderplatz, East Berlin - largest demonstration in GDR history

### Fall of the Berlin Wall (November 9, 1989)

**Events:**
- Politburo decided to ease travel restrictions
- Spokesperson Gunter Schabowski announced new regulations at press conference
- Asked when regulations take effect, answered "immediately, without delay"
- Thousands gathered at border crossings
- Overwhelmed guards opened checkpoints
- Citizens began physically dismantling Wall with sledgehammers

**Significance:**
- End of physical division of Berlin after 28 years
- Symbol of Cold War's end
- Precipitated rapid collapse of GDR

---

## Fall / End

### Collapse of SED Rule (October-December 1989)

- **October 18**: Honecker replaced by Egon Krenz
- **November 9**: Berlin Wall opened
- **November 13**: Hans Modrow became Prime Minister, began limited reforms
- **December 1**: Parliament removed SED's constitutional leading role
- **December 3**: SED Politburo and Central Committee resigned en masse
- **December 7**: Round Table discussions began with opposition groups

### Free Elections (March 18, 1990)

- First (and last) free elections in GDR history
- Alliance for Germany (Christian Democrat-led coalition) won unexpectedly large victory
- Mandate for rapid reunification with West Germany
- Lothar de Maiziere became final Prime Minister

### Path to Reunification

- **July 1, 1990**: Economic and monetary union; West German Deutschmark became GDR currency
- **August 31, 1990**: Unification Treaty signed
- **October 3, 1990**: German reunification; GDR ceased to exist
- Five eastern states (Lander) joined Federal Republic of Germany

## Aftermath

### Immediate Transition Challenges

- Mass unemployment as uncompetitive GDR enterprises closed
- "Solidarity surcharge" tax on West Germans to fund reconstruction
- Brain drain as young, educated East Germans moved west
- Property restitution disputes

### The Stasi Files

**Preservation:**
- When Wall fell (November 1989), Stasi ordered to destroy evidence
- January 1990: Activists occupied Stasi headquarters, stopped destruction
- 45 million+ pages already torn apart
- 15,000+ bags containing 600 million+ paper pieces recovered

**Access:**
- Stasi Records Act passed (December 1991)
- Joachim Gauck (later German President) became first Federal Commissioner for Stasi Records
- Citizens first able to view their files January 2, 1992
- By 2019: 2.75 million people had requested to see their files

**Lustration:**
- Public officials, journalists, teachers vetted for Stasi collaboration
- Many careers ended by revelations of informant activity
- Continuing controversy over former collaborators in public life

### Trials and Prosecutions

**Legal Challenges:**
- Difficulty prosecuting crimes that were legal under GDR law
- "Victors' justice" concerns
- Limited statutes of limitations

**Notable Cases:**
- **Erich Mielke**: Convicted (1993) for 1931 police murders, not Stasi crimes; sentence reduced on appeal
- **Egon Krenz**: Convicted (1997) for Wall deaths; served partial sentence
- **Border Guard Trials**: Some guards convicted for shooting escapees
- **Karl-Heinz Knoche & Heinz Kadgien**: Judge and prosecutor sentenced (1998) for 1981 execution of Stasi officer Werner Teske

**Limitations:**
- Many perpetrators never prosecuted
- Sentences generally light
- Debate continues over adequacy of accountability

### Long-Term Effects

**Economic:**
- Massive public investment in eastern Germany (over 2 trillion euros by 2019)
- Persistent economic gap between eastern and western states
- Higher unemployment in east (though gap narrowing)

**Social:**
- Surveys show some East Germans nostalgic for certain aspects of GDR (job security, community)
- "Ostalgie" phenomenon (nostalgia for East)
- Generational differences in memory and interpretation

**Political:**
- Far-right AfD party shows strongest support in eastern states
- Legacy of distrust affects political culture
- Die Linke party (successor to SED) remains significant force

**Psychological:**
- Research shows Stasi surveillance led to lower trust, reduced social activity in affected areas
- Effects persisted through 1990s and 2000s
- Higher unemployment and out-migration in areas with more intense surveillance

## Death Toll Estimates

### Berlin Wall Deaths (1961-1989)

| Category | Number |
|----------|--------|
| Total deaths at Berlin Wall | At least 140 |
| Shot, accidents, suicides while fleeing | 101 |
| East/West civilians not fleeing (shot or accidents) | 30 |
| Soviet soldier | 1 |
| On-duty border guards killed | 8 |
| Deaths at Berlin border checkpoints | 251 |

- Of 140 total: 91 victims shot by border soldiers
- First victim: Ida Siekmann (August 22, 1961) - fell from apartment window
- Last person shot fleeing: Chris Gueffroy (February 1989)
- Nearly 86% (120 people) died between 1961-1975

### Inner-German Border Deaths

- Over 100,000 attempted escape (1961-1988)
- Over 600 killed by border guards or died in escape attempts
- Current research: At least 260-327 deaths
- Methods of death: shot, drowned, accidents, suicides upon capture

### Political Repression Deaths and Victims

| Category | Estimate |
|----------|----------|
| Political prisoners (total over regime) | 200,000-250,000 |
| Died in Soviet internment camps (1945-1950) | ~65,000 |
| Executed by Soviet courts post-WWII | 756 |
| Total estimated killed by communist rulers | Up to 100,000 |

## Scholarly Debate

### The Totalitarianism vs. Normality Debate

Central historiographical controversy: Was the GDR primarily a totalitarian dictatorship or did "normal" life exist alongside repression?

**Totalitarianism Perspective:**
- Emphasizes Stasi surveillance, political repression, lack of freedom
- Points to border killings, political prisoners, forced conformity
- Views GDR as fundamentally illegitimate state

**"Normality" Perspective (Social History Approach):**
- Mary Fulbrook's concept of the "People's State"
- Examines how ordinary people navigated system
- Emphasizes lived experience beyond pure repression
- Questions whether "totalitarianism" captures full complexity

### Extent of Stasi Penetration

**Key Questions:**
- Did Stasi's enormous size reflect strength or weakness of system?
- How deeply did surveillance actually penetrate society?
- What was the nature and extent of collaboration?

**Findings:**
- Contrary to popular perception of universal surveillance, informant distribution was uneven
- Majority of informants were SED members, nomenklatura cadres, military/police staff
- Church and opposition milieus heavily penetrated but represented minority
- Jens Gieseke: Leading historian on Stasi matters
- Gary Bruce's *The Firm*: Examines Stasi integration into small-town society

### Regime Legitimacy

**Debates:**
- Did GDR possess any genuine legitimacy beyond coercion?
- Role of socialist ideals in maintaining system
- Significance of social achievements (education, healthcare, gender equality) vs. political repression
- Why did regime collapse so quickly in 1989?

### Historiographical Waves

**First Wave (1990s):**
- Focus on power, repression, Stasi
- Spectacular events (1953 uprising, Wall)
- "Sensational search" for former Stasi informants
- Criticized as sometimes resembling belated denazification

**Second Wave (2000s onwards):**
- Social and cultural history approaches
- Everyday life, gender, consumption
- More nuanced examination of complicity and resistance
- Attempts to understand society from inside, not just top-down

### Stasi Research as Public History

- Always intertwined with public memory and politics
- Research perceived by some as Western project to delegitimize socialist alternative
- Tensions between scholarly analysis and public/victim-centered narratives
- Question of how to balance understanding perpetrators with respecting victims
