## Key Figures

### Mohamed Siad Barre (1910-1995)
- Born in 1910 near Shilabo in the Ogaden region (now Ethiopia)
- Orphaned young; worked as herder before joining Italian colonial police in 1935
- Served in colonial gendarmerie during Italian rule
- After independence (1960), rose through ranks of Somali National Army
- Appointed commander of Somali National Army in 1966
- Led bloodless coup d'etat on 21 October 1969
- Chairman of the Supreme Revolutionary Council (1969-1976)
- President and General Secretary of Somali Revolutionary Socialist Party (1976-1991)
- Known as "Guulwade" (Victorious Leader) in state propaganda
- Overthrown January 1991; fled to Nigeria via Kenya
- Died 2 January 1995 in Lagos, Nigeria, of heart attack

### Mohamed Ali Samantar (1931-2016)
- Vice President of Somalia (1971-1986)
- Minister of Defense (1971-1980)
- Prime Minister (1987-1990)
- Held rank of Major General; considered second most powerful person in Somalia
- Oversaw military campaigns that resulted in mass atrocities against Isaaq civilians
- Fled to United States after regime's collapse
- Found liable in U.S. court for war crimes and human rights abuses in 2012
- Ordered to pay $21 million in damages to Somali victims
- Died 19 August 2016 in the United States

### Abdi Aden Magan
- Colonel; Head of NSS Department of Investigations (1988-1990)
- Ran the notorious interrogation centers where thousands were tortured
- Found liable for torture and arbitrary detention in U.S. federal court
- Key figure in systematic repression of political opponents

### Mohamed Siad Hersi "Morgan"
- General; Siad Barre's son-in-law
- Commander of northern military operations
- Primary architect of the Hargeisa bombardment and Isaaq genocide campaigns
- Nicknamed "Butcher of Hargeisa"
- Authored internal military report recommending clan-based extermination policies

### Other Notable Figures
- **Ahmed Suleiman Abdalle** - President of the Supreme Revolutionary Council (ceremonial after 1976)
- **Omar Arteh Ghalib** - Foreign Minister, later Prime Minister
- **Jama Mohamed Ghalib** - Interior Minister and head of National Security Service at various points

## Pre-Conditions (1960-1969)

### Colonial Legacy
- Somalia formed from union of former British Somaliland (north) and Italian Somaliland (south) in 1960
- Colonial borders divided ethnic Somali populations across five territories
- Pan-Somali nationalism ("Greater Somalia") sought unification of all Somali-inhabited regions
- Including Ogaden (Ethiopia), Northern Frontier District (Kenya), and Djibouti

### Post-Colonial Democracy (1960-1969)
- Somalia initially established parliamentary democracy
- First President: Aden Abdullah Osman (1960-1967)
- Second President: Abdirashid Ali Shermarke (1967-1969)
- Multi-party system with clan-based political affiliations
- Growing corruption and mismanagement of public funds
- Clan politics increasingly dominated government

### Immediate Triggers

#### Assassination of President Shermarke (15 October 1969)
- President Abdirashid Shermarke shot dead in Las Anod by his own bodyguard
- Assassin: Said Yusuf Ismail, a policeman from a rival sub-clan
- Occurred while Shermarke toured drought-stricken northern regions
- Some scholars suggest Soviet and/or military involvement in assassination

#### Political Vacuum
- Parliament deadlocked over succession
- Prime Minister Mohamed Egal unable to maintain control
- Widespread public dissatisfaction with corruption
- Military viewed as only institution capable of restoring order

## Rise to Power

### The 1969 Coup d'Etat

#### Events of 21 October 1969
- Six days after Shermarke's assassination
- Coup launched in early morning hours during presidential funeral period
- Bloodless military takeover
- Troops sealed key locations: Parliament, Radio Mogadishu, police headquarters, Prime Minister's residence
- Acting President Sheikh Mukhtar Hussein and Prime Minister Mohamed Egal detained

#### Immediate Actions
- Constitution suspended
- National Assembly dissolved
- Supreme Court abolished
- Political parties banned
- Country renamed Somali Democratic Republic

### Supreme Revolutionary Council (SRC)

#### Structure
- 25-member military junta established
- Siad Barre named President and Chairman
- Initially collegial decision-making
- Power gradually concentrated in Barre's hands

### Adoption of Scientific Socialism

#### Ideological Framework
- Marxism-Leninism adapted to Somali and Islamic context
- Three components (according to Barre):
  1. Community development based on self-reliance
  2. Socialism based on Marxist principles
  3. Islamic ideology
- Barre argued: "No chapter, not even a single word, in our Koran opposes scientific socialism"

#### Soviet Alliance
- Signed Treaty of Friendship and Cooperation with USSR (1974)
- Soviet military advisers trained Somali armed forces
- KGB helped establish National Security Service
- Massive Soviet military aid including tanks, aircraft, weapons

### Early Reforms (1969-1977)

#### Positive Achievements
- **Literacy Campaign**: Literacy rate increased from 5% (1972) to 24% (1990)
- **Somali Script**: Latin-based writing system officially adopted (1972)
- **Education**: Students sent to rural areas to teach literacy
- **Nationalization**: Banks and major industries nationalized
- **Modernization**: Public works programs, infrastructure development
- **Women's Rights**: Some progressive legislation on gender equality

#### Early Warning Signs
- Personality cult around Barre developed quickly
- Portraits displayed everywhere alongside Marx and Lenin
- Title "Guulwade" (Victorious Leader) made mandatory
- "Jalle" (comrade) required form of address
- Political opponents imprisoned or killed

## Methods of Control

### National Security Service (NSS)

#### Structure and Purpose
- Established 1970; modeled on Soviet KGB
- Primary intelligence and secret police agency
- Known as "Hay'ada Nabadsugida Qaranka" (HNQ)
- Also called "Black SS" or "Somali Gestapo" by critics

#### Methods
- Extensive network of informers throughout society
- Arbitrary arrests and indefinite detention
- Systematic torture of political prisoners
- Extrajudicial executions

#### Notorious Facilities
- **"Godka" (The Hole)**: NSS headquarters and interrogation center in Mogadishu
  - Underground cells
  - Permanent solitary confinement
  - Systematic torture
- **Mogadishu Central Prison**
- **Lanta Bur**, **Labtanjirow**, **Burwein** detention centers

#### Documented Abuses
- Routine torture of political prisoners
- Thousands detained without charge or trial
- Grossly unfair political trials resulting in executions
- Extrajudicial executions of thousands of civilians
- Prisoners kept incommunicado without medical treatment

### Clan Manipulation

#### The M.O.D. System
- Despite public anti-tribalism rhetoric, Barre entrenched clan favoritism
- Inner circle dominated by three clan groups (M.O.D.):
  - **M**arehan: Barre's own clan
  - **O**gaden: Barre's mother's clan
  - **D**hulbahante: Barre's son-in-law's clan
- Key government and military positions reserved for M.O.D. clans

#### Clan-Based Collective Punishment
- Entire clans punished for actions of individual members
- Targeting of Majeerteen clan (1979-1980): 2,000+ killed
- Targeting of Isaaq clan (1987-1989): 50,000-200,000 killed
- Sieges and blockades of clan-inhabited areas
- Destruction of subsistence infrastructure (water reservoirs, livestock)

### Propaganda and Ideology

#### Personality Cult
- Barre's image displayed in all public buildings and offices
- Massive hand-painted posters throughout cities
- State media constant glorification of "Father of the Nation"
- Mandatory political education sessions

#### Control of Information
- State monopoly on media
- Severe censorship
- Imprisonment of journalists and critics

### Somali Revolutionary Socialist Party (SRSP)

#### Establishment (July 1976)
- SRC disbanded and replaced by SRSP
- Sole legal political party
- Attempted fusion of Marxism and Islam
- All political activity channeled through party structures

## Key Events

### The Ogaden War (July 1977 - March 1978)

#### Background
- Ogaden region of Ethiopia inhabited by ethnic Somalis
- Part of Greater Somalia irredentist ambitions
- Ethiopian Revolution (1974) created opportunity
- Western Somali Liberation Front (WSLF) sought annexation to Somalia

#### Invasion
- **12 July 1977**: Somalia invaded Ethiopia
- 35,000 Somali National Army troops plus 15,000 WSLF fighters
- Initial rapid success: 60% of Ogaden captured by end of July
- By October 1977: 90% of Ogaden (320,000 sq km) under Somali control

#### Superpower Realignment
- USSR faced dilemma: allied with both Somalia and new Ethiopian Derg regime
- Soviets chose Ethiopia as larger strategic prize
- **September 1977**: USSR terminated arms deliveries to Somalia
- **November 1977**: Somalia abrogated Soviet treaty, expelled advisors
- Many Soviet advisors went directly to Ethiopia with knowledge of Somali military

#### Ethiopian Counteroffensive
- Massive Soviet airlift: $1 billion+ in military aid
- 16,000-18,000 Cuban troops deployed
- 1,500 Soviet military advisors
- Joint Ethiopian-Cuban-Soviet command established January 1978
- Battle of Harar (October 1977-January 1978): Turning point
- **March 1978**: Ethiopia recaptured virtually all Ogaden territory

#### Consequences for Somalia
- Devastating military defeat
- Over 8,000 Somali soldiers killed
- One-third of army destroyed
- Hundreds of thousands of refugees flooded into Somalia
- Economic devastation
- Delegitimization of Barre regime

### Switch from Soviet to US Alignment (1977-1980)

#### Strategic Realignment
- Ogaden War ended Soviet-Somali alliance
- United States saw opportunity in Cold War context
- **1980**: US-Somalia access agreement signed
- US gained use of Berbera port (former Soviet base)
- Somalia received American military and economic aid

#### Continuation of Authoritarian Rule
- Switch in superpower patrons did not alter domestic repression
- Human rights abuses continued and escalated
- US strategic interests prioritized over human rights concerns
- Aid continued despite documented atrocities

### The 1978 Coup Attempt

#### Failed Putsch (April 1978)
- Military officers attempted coup following Ogaden defeat
- Coup quickly suppressed
- Participants executed
- Triggered formation of armed opposition movements

#### Emergence of Opposition
- **SSDF (Somali Salvation Democratic Front)**: Formed 1978
  - Based on Majeerteen clan
  - Founded by Colonel Abdullahi Yusuf Ahmed
  - Operated from Ethiopia
- **SNM (Somali National Movement)**: Formed 1981
  - Based on Isaaq clan
  - Founded in London by dissidents
  - Headquarters moved to Ethiopia 1982

### Clan Conflicts and Collective Punishment

#### Anti-Majeerteen Campaign (1979-1980)
- Collective punishment for SSDF rebellion
- Presidential Guard killed 2,000+ Majeerteen civilians in May-June 1979
- Sieges and blockades of Majeerteen areas
- Destruction of water reservoirs and cattle
- Schools and health facilities closed

#### Anti-Isaaq Campaigns (1981-1991)
- Escalating repression of Isaaq clan following SNM formation
- Economic marginalization of northern regions
- Arbitrary arrests, torture, disappearances
- Culminated in full-scale genocide (1987-1989)

### The Isaaq Genocide (1987-1989)

#### Context
- **April 1988**: Peace accord between Barre and Mengistu (Ethiopia)
- Both agreed to stop harboring each other's dissidents
- SNM forced to relocate from Ethiopia into northern Somalia
- **May 1988**: SNM launched major offensive, briefly capturing Hargeisa and Burao

#### Government Response
- Full-scale military assault on Isaaq civilian populations
- Aerial bombardment of Hargeisa and Burao
- Ground attacks by Somali National Army
- Targeting of Isaaq civilians regardless of SNM affiliation

#### Methods
- Aerial bombing and strafing of towns and villages
- Estimated 70% of Hargeisa destroyed
- Artillery bombardment of civilian areas
- Extrajudicial executions and mass killings
- Rape used as weapon of war
- Destruction of property and infrastructure

#### Death Toll
- Conservative estimates: 50,000-100,000 killed
- Local reports: up to 200,000 Isaaq civilians killed
- Hargeisa: 5,000-40,000+ civilians killed in bombardment alone
- Earned nickname "Dresden of Africa"

#### Mass Graves and Refugee Crisis
- 226 known mass graves documented in Somaliland
- As many as 200,000 buried in mass graves
- 300,000+ refugees fled to Ethiopia
- World's largest refugee crisis at the time

#### Legal Determination
- UN investigation concluded the killings constituted genocide
- Found to be "conceived, planned and perpetrated by the Somali government against the Isaaq people"

## Fall

### Collapse of the Regime (1989-1991)

#### Erosion of Support
- End of Cold War reduced Somalia's strategic importance
- US and Western aid diminished
- Economic collapse
- Military fragmented along clan lines
- Multiple armed opposition movements active

#### Proliferation of Opposition Movements
- **SNM** (Isaaq): Controlled much of northwest by late 1980s
- **SSDF** (Majeerteen): Active in central regions
- **USC (United Somali Congress)**: Formed late 1980s by Hawiye clans
  - **Ali Mahdi Mohamed**: Abgaal sub-clan faction
  - **Mohamed Farah Aidid**: Habr Gedir sub-clan faction
- **SPM (Somali Patriotic Movement)**: Formed April 1989 by Ogadeni officers after Barre purged Defense Minister

#### Final Collapse
- By late 1990, government controlled only Mogadishu and limited surrounding area
- USC forces advanced on capital
- **26 January 1991**: Rebels overran Mogadishu Airport and Presidential Palace
- Barre fled Mogadishu in tank convoy

### Barre in Exile
- Initially retreated to southern Somalia, attempted comeback
- Fighting continued until 1992
- Fled to Kenya, then Nigeria
- Granted asylum by Nigerian government
- Died 2 January 1995 in Lagos of heart attack

---

## Aftermath

### Immediate Chaos (1991-1992)
- Power vacuum triggered clan-based warfare
- No central government established
- "Burbur" (catastrophe) period: December 1991 - March 1992

#### Warlord Era
- Multiple factions competed for control
- **Ali Mahdi Mohamed**: Declared himself president (not recognized)
- **Mohamed Farah Aidid**: Controlled southern Mogadishu, rejected Ali Mahdi
- Mogadishu divided between rival warlords

#### Human Cost
- 25,000 killed in Mogadishu fighting alone (1991-1992)
- 1.5 million refugees fled the country
- 2+ million internally displaced
- Famine conditions developed

### International Intervention

#### UNOSOM I (1992)
- UN peacekeeping mission
- Limited effectiveness

#### UNITAF / Operation Restore Hope (December 1992-May 1993)
- US-led intervention
- Humanitarian mission to deliver food aid
- 28,000 US troops deployed

#### UNOSOM II (May 1993-March 1995)
- UN took over from US
- Mission expanded to nation-building
- **Battle of Mogadishu (October 1993)**: 18 US soldiers killed
- US withdrawal followed
- UN mission ended without achieving objectives

### Long-Term Consequences

#### State Collapse
- Somalia became paradigmatic example of "failed state"
- Term "failed state" originated in 1990s largely in reference to Somalia
- No effective central government for over two decades
- Transitional Federal Government established 2004 but remained weak

#### Somaliland Independence
- **May 1991**: SNM declared independence of Republic of Somaliland
- Former British Somaliland territory
- Maintained relative stability and democratic governance
- Not internationally recognized as of 2024

#### Al-Shabaab and Islamist Insurgency
- Power vacuum enabled rise of Islamist groups
- Islamic Courts Union briefly controlled Mogadishu (2006)
- Al-Shabaab emerged as dominant insurgent force
- Ongoing conflict continues

#### Current Status
- Federal Government of Somalia established 2012
- African Union peacekeeping mission (AMISOM/ATMIS) provides security
- Al-Shabaab insurgency ongoing
- Somalia consistently ranked among world's most fragile states

## Scholarly Debates

### Was Barre Responsible for Creating a Failed State?

#### Direct Responsibility Argument
- Barre destroyed democratic institutions immediately upon taking power
- Clan manipulation entrenched divisions that erupted after his fall
- Security apparatus trained in repression, not legitimate governance
- Economic mismanagement left country impoverished
- Military defeats (Ogaden War) delegitimized state institutions

#### Structural/Colonial Factors Argument
- Colonial borders artificially divided Somali nation
- Cold War superpowers enabled and sustained Barre's regime
- International aid withdrawal precipitated collapse
- Clan system predated Barre; he exploited but did not create it

#### Synthesis View (Ingiriis, "The Suicidal State")
- "Colonialism left Africa on its own space wherein African leaders could have made a difference"
- Contemporary conflicts attributable to "past plunders of post-colonial leaders trained by colonial authorities"
- Barre's regime was "suicidal" in that its methods guaranteed eventual collapse

### Classification of Isaaq Killings

#### Genocide Determination
- UN investigation concluded killings met definition of genocide
- Systematic targeting based on clan/ethnic identity
- State-planned and executed
- Intent to destroy group as such

#### Counter-Arguments
- Some argue political/military rather than ethnic motivation
- SNM insurgency provided justification (however disproportionate response)
- Debates over precise death toll

### Cold War Responsibility

#### Superpower Complicity
- Both USSR (1969-1977) and USA (1978-1991) provided massive aid
- Strategic interests prioritized over human rights
- Weapons and training enabled atrocities
- Aid withdrawal contributed to collapse

#### Continuing Relevance
- Debates over foreign intervention responsibilities
- Questions about accountability for enabling authoritarian regimes

### Lessons for State-Building

#### What Somalia's Collapse Teaches
- Clan/ethnic manipulation for political gain has long-term consequences
- Predatory governance destroys state legitimacy
- Security services become sources of insecurity
- Military capability without political legitimacy is unsustainable
