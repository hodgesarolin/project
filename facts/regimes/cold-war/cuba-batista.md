# Cuba Under Fulgencio Batista (1933-1944, 1952-1959)

## Overview
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Country/Region** | Cuba |
| **Dates** | First Period: September 1933 - October 1944; Second Period: March 10, 1952 - January 1, 1959 |
| **Leader(s)** | Fulgencio Batista y Zaldivar (born Ruben Zaldivar; January 16, 1901 - August 6, 1973) |
| **Type** | Military dictatorship / Personalist (first period more benign; second period brutal dictatorship) |
| **Duration** | First period: ~11 years; Second period: ~7 years (Total influence: ~18 years) |
| **Estimated Death Toll** | Disputed: 746-20,000 (second period only). JFK claimed 20,000 in campaign speech; Bohemia documented 746; historians commonly cite ~4,000; Havana morgue recorded 700+ torture victims 1952-1958 |

## Key Figures

### Inner Circle
- **Fulgencio Batista** - President and dictator
- **Marta Fernandez Miranda de Batista** - Second wife, First Lady (1952-1959)
- **Gonzalo Guell** - Prime Minister; arranged Batista's exile to Dominican Republic

### Military Leadership
- **General Eulogio Cantillo** - Military commander who negotiated with Castro; led 10,000 troops in Operation Verano
- **General Francisco Tabernilla** - Army Chief of Staff; fled to Jacksonville after revolution
- **General Martin Tamayo** - Issued "ten-for-one" execution order after Moncada attack
- **Colonel Ramon Barquin** - Led failed 1956 coup attempt (Conspiracy of the Pure)

### Security Apparatus
- **Mariano Faget Diaz** - Director of BRAC (1956); described as "a technician of torture"
- **Jose de Jesus Castano** - BRAC agent; captured and executed by Castro's forces
- **Pilar Garcia** - National Police Chief; described as "worst of the terrorists"

### Political Allies
- **Andres Rivero Aguero** - Puppet President-elect (never took office)
- **Carlos Rafael Rodriguez** - Minister Without Portfolio (during Communist Party cooperation period)
- **Rafael Leonidas Trujillo** - Dominican Republic dictator; provided initial asylum for Batista

### Organized Crime Associates
- **Meyer Lansky** - "The Mafia's financier"; mastermind of casino empire in Cuba
- **Charles "Lucky" Luciano** - Organized 1946 Havana Conference
- **Santo Trafficante Jr.** - Tampa crime boss with major Havana holdings

## Classification
Batista's rule qualifies as authoritarian/dictatorial based on:

**First Period (1933-1944):**
- Ruled through puppet presidents from 1933-1940
- Self-appointed Army Chief of Staff after Sergeants' Revolt
- Controlled government through military power rather than democratic mandate
- However: enacted progressive 1940 Constitution; held free elections in 1944; allowed peaceful transfer of power

**Second Period (1952-1959):**
- Seized power through military coup, canceling scheduled elections
- Suspended the 1940 Constitution
- Revoked civil liberties including right to strike
- Established brutal secret police (BRAC, SIM)
- Rigged 1954 and 1958 elections as sole candidate
- Systematic use of torture, political murder, and public executions
- Close ties with organized crime; massive personal corruption

## Pre-Conditions

### The Machado Dictatorship (1925-1933)
- Gerardo Machado y Morales initially popular president
- Violated promise to serve one term; manipulated 1928 re-election
- Economic crisis: Great Depression devastated Cuba's sugar-dependent economy
- Repressive tactics: curtailed free speech, used police violence against opponents
- "Unleashed a wave of violence against his critics, with numerous murders and assassinations committed by the police and army"
- Described as a dictator by historians

### The 1933 Revolution
- Popular uprising combined students, labor unions, and military enlisted men
- U.S. mediation failed; Machado fled in August 1933
- Provisional government of Carlos Manuel de Cespedes installed
- Deep political instability; economic crisis continued
- Power vacuum created opportunity for military intervention

### Cuban Society Pre-Batista
- Highly stratified society with significant inequality
- Sugar economy dominated by U.S. interests
- Strong labor movement and student political activism
- History of U.S. intervention under Platt Amendment (1901-1934)
- Military as institution looking for expanded political role

## Rise to Power

### First Rise: The Sergeants' Revolt (September 4-5, 1933)
- Batista was a sergeant and military tribunal stenographer
- "Not originally the leader of the movement"; joined because "he was the only sergeant who had a car"
- Allied with students and labor leaders against provisional government
- Revolt built on grievances of enlisted men about their treatment
- Overthrew Carlos Manuel de Cespedes's provisional government
- Proclaimed program of economic restructuring, justice, and democracy

**Immediate Aftermath:**
- Established "pentarchy" (five-member executive commission)
- Ramon Grau San Martin selected as president
- Batista self-appointed as Army Chief of Staff with rank of Colonel
- Effectively controlled the collective government through military power

**Rule Through Puppets (1933-1940):**
- "Using the army as his base of support, Batista ruled Cuba indirectly through a series of puppet presidents"
- Consolidated control through patronage rather than terror
- "An astute judge of men"

**Elected Presidency (1940-1944):**
- Resigned from Army in December 1939 to run for president
- Formed coalition with Cuban Communist Party
- Won election in October 1940
- Drafted progressive 1940 Constitution
- Presided over Cuba's support for Allies in WWII
- Term-limited; succeeded by Grau after free 1944 elections
- Retired to Daytona Beach, Florida
- Returned to Cuba 1948; elected to Senate

### Second Rise: The 1952 Coup (March 10, 1952)
- Running distant third in three-way presidential race
- Roberto Agramonte (Orthodox Party) leading in polls
- "Distrusting his prospects of winning, organised the second successful coup of his career"
- Seized power at Camp Columbia, army headquarters outside Havana
- Coup took "altogether precisely one hour and seventeen minutes"
- United States recognized his government on March 27, 1952
- Suspended 1940 Constitution; revoked civil liberties
- "When in March 1952 Batista, in a coup d'etat, destroyed the democratic republic he had brought into existence, the stage was set for revolution"

## Methods of Control

### Propaganda/Media Control
- Controlled the university, the press, and the Congress
- Tighter censorship of media as opposition grew
- Cuban magazine Bohemia secretly kept tally of torture victims
- "The brutal methods of the regime were unreported in the Havana press"

### Secret Police/Surveillance

**Bureau for the Repression of Communist Activities (BRAC)**
- Established May 4, 1955 with CIA assistance
- CIA Director Allen Dulles praised BRAC as "a great step forward in the cause of Freedom"
- CIA offered to train officers and show them "the techniques used"
- Directed by Mariano Faget Diaz from 1956
- "Gained a reputation for brutality in its fight against the 26th of July Movement"
- Three-stage interrogation: "persuasive talk, before moving on to psychological and then physical torture"
- "BRAC agents spent the best part of four years brutally torturing and killing Batista's opponents"

**Military Intelligence Service (SIM)**
- Worked in close collaboration with BRAC
- Tasked with surveillance of suspected communists
- Infiltrated unions and opposition movements
- American officials noted SIM was "used as a weapon against all opposition"
- "Violence was described as characteristic of the SIM"

**Other Security Services:**
- Naval Intelligence Service (SIN)
- Maritime Police
- Bureau of Investigations
- National Police
- Rural Guard
- These were described as "true academies of torture and death"

**Escuadron 41**
- Colonial fortress in Matanzas
- By 1958, converted to torture and detention center

### Political Repression
- Suspended right to strike
- Rigged elections in 1954 and 1958; ran as sole candidate
- Opponents jailed, tortured, or killed
- "Hundreds of mangled bodies were left hanging from lamp posts or dumped in the streets"
- Public executions of suspects including youth "as a warning to others"
- Bodies dumped in Pinar del Rio hills, mass graves, abandoned sites
- Magazine Bohemia estimated "as many as ten a week were killed in Havana alone"

### Cult of Personality
- Less developed than contemporary dictatorships
- More focused on image of stability and anti-communism
- Presented as defender against Castro's rebels
- Emphasized alliance with United States

### Economic Control
- "Aligned with the wealthiest landowners who owned the largest sugar plantations"
- "Presided over a stagnating economy that widened the gap between rich and poor Cubans"
- Personal enrichment through "sophisticated practices of corruption"
- "Embezzled huge sums from the soaring economy"
- Casino licensing required $250,000+ in under-the-table fees
- Left Cuba with personal fortune estimated at $300 million

### Military/Violence
- Army loyal through patronage and promotion
- Operation Verano: 10,000 troops deployed against Sierra Maestra rebels
- "Ten-for-one" law after Moncada: execute 10 rebels for every soldier killed
- Rural Guard used for countryside pacification
- Systematic torture in military and police facilities

## Key Events & Atrocities

### The Moncada Barracks Attack and Aftermath (July 26, 1953)
**The Attack:**
- 136 rebels led by Fidel Castro attacked second largest military garrison
- Attack began at 5:15 AM in Santiago de Cuba
- Rebels defeated; "widely accepted as the beginning of the Cuban Revolution"
- 5 rebels killed in actual combat

**The Atrocities:**
- 56 rebels executed by Batista regime after capture
- "Half of the rebels died, not in combat, but under torture"
- 18 rebels captured at Civil Hospital executed within 2 hours at target range
- "Their corpses were strewn throughout the garrison to simulate death in combat"
- 34 rebels captured over next 3 days murdered after interrogation
- General Tamayo issued order to "kill ten rebels for every soldier killed"

**Notable Torture Cases:**
- Abel Santamaria: Eyes gouged out in front of his sister Haydee
- Boris Luis Santa Coloma: "Genitalia dismembered"
- Both "heinously tortured to death by Batista's henchmen"

**Impact:**
- "The Castro legend started only through what is regarded by local residents as an act of brutality"
- Castro sentenced to 15 years; released after 2 years in amnesty
- Date became name of revolutionary movement (M-26-7)

### Systematic Torture (1952-1958)
**Documentation:**
- Havana morgue director: 700+ bodies with evidence of severe torture
- Bohemia published three issues in January-February 1959 detailing torture chronology
- New York Times reporter Herbert L. Matthews: "Batista's brutal system of torture" was "the most horrific in Latin American history"

**Methods:**
- Physical torture in detention centers
- Psychological torture
- Public executions
- Bodies displayed on lamp posts
- Dumping in mass graves

**Scale:**
- JFK (1960): "Fulgencio Batista murdered 20,000 Cubans in seven years"
- CIA estimates: 20,000 deaths
- Most historians: approximately 4,000 deaths
- Bohemia investigation: 746 documented deaths
- Batista called 20,000 figure a "Formidable Lie"

### Key Massacres During Dictatorship
- Multiple documented instances of group executions
- "Murders fell under the category of 'massacres' because it wasn't only one person killed but several at a time"
- Rural Guard particularly implicated in countryside killings

## U.S. Relations and Organized Crime Connections

### U.S. Government Relations

**Support Phase (1952-1958):**
- U.S. recognized coup government within 17 days (March 27, 1952)
- Military assistance: $1.5 million annually 1954-1956; doubled 1957-1958
- U.S. military mission trained Batista's army
- CIA helped establish BRAC (May 1955)
- CIA Director Allen Dulles personally praised BRAC
- Ambassador Earl E.T. Smith supportive of Batista

**Reassessment and Arms Embargo:**
- Growing Congressional and public opposition to supporting Batista
- Recognition that Batista "probably did not command the support of a majority"
- Arms embargo imposed March 14, 1958
- "Washington made the critical move in March 1958 to end sales of rifles to Batista's forces, thus changing the course of the Cuban Revolution irreversibly"
- Ambassador Smith pressured Batista to step down in December 1958
- U.S. recognized new Cuban government January 7, 1959

**Scholarly Assessment:**
- Eisenhower was "not 'supporting' the dictatorship, but decreasingly tolerating it"

### Organized Crime Empire

**Origins of Mafia-Batista Alliance (1933):**
- Connection initiated after Sergeants' Revolt
- Meyer Lansky presented Batista with "multiple suitcases filled with cash"
- Batista guaranteed $3-5 million annually in exchange for gambling monopoly
- "The creation of a criminal empire as such began toward the end of 1933"
- Operated under direct orders of Lucky Luciano

**The 1946 Havana Conference:**
- "Most important US organized crime summit since the 1929 Atlantic City Conference"
- Held at Hotel Nacional de Cuba, December 22, 1946
- Organized by Lansky on Luciano's orders
- Determined Syndicate policy for postwar era
- Luciano purchased $150,000 interest in Hotel Nacional

**Casino Operations:**
- 1955: Batista changed gambling laws to allow casinos in hotels worth $1 million+
- Government received $25,000 license fee plus 20% of profits
- Under-the-table fees of $250,000+ required for licenses
- Key properties: Hotel Nacional, Hotel Capri, Hotel Riviera, Sans Souci
- "The Syndicate boys were the only ones who knew enough to run the casinos at a profit"
- "They were considered respectable businessmen in Havana"

**Scope of Corruption:**
- "The mob funneled dirty money into Cuba to build casinos and hotels"
- Funds facilitated corrupt political system
- Periodic payoffs to politicians
- "When Castro came to power, the image of Havana was as the center of graft, corruption, vice and the Mafia" (Wayne Smith, former U.S. official)

**Key Figures:**
- Meyer Lansky: "Top dog in the legitimate gambling racket"; lost estimated $7 million when Castro nationalized casinos
- Santo Trafficante Jr.: Major casino operator
- Kefauver Committee identified Lansky as "one of the top ten racketeers in the country"

## Opposition & Resistance

### Revolutionary Opposition
- **26th of July Movement (M-26-7):** Founded by Fidel Castro after Moncada; primary revolutionary force
- **Student movements:** Key allies in both 1933 revolt and 1950s opposition
- **Labor unions:** Despite suppression, maintained resistance
- **Orthodox Party:** Democratic opposition; Castro was former member
- **Authentic Party:** Traditional opposition party

### Military Opposition
- **Conspiracy of the Pure (1956):** Led by Colonel Ramon Barquin; failed coup attempt by career officers
- **General Eulogio Cantillo:** Secretly met with Castro December 1958; agreed to arrest Batista

### International Pressure
- Growing U.S. Congressional opposition
- Latin American criticism
- Media exposure (Herbert Matthews, New York Times)

## Fall / End

### The Cuban Revolution (1956-1959)
- Castro returned from Mexican exile, December 1956
- Landed with 82 men on yacht Granma
- Established base in Sierra Maestra mountains
- Guerrilla warfare gradually expanded

### Final Offensive and Collapse
**Operation Verano (Summer 1958):**
- 10,000 government troops surrounded Sierra Maestra
- Rebels repulsed offensive; government morale collapsed

**December 1958:**
- Battle of Yaguajay: Camilo Cienfuegos victory, December 30
- Battle of Santa Clara: Che Guevara's forces captured city, December 31
- "News of these defeats caused Batista to panic"

**Batista's Flight (January 1, 1959):**
- Around midnight, informed cabinet he was resigning
- "For the salvation of the republic, the military forces have decided that it is necessary for General Batista to withdraw"
- 3:00 AM: Boarded plane with 40 supporters and family
- Fled to Ciudad Trujillo, Dominican Republic
- Two additional planes followed with ministers and officials
- "Batista took along a personal fortune of more than $300 million"

### Castro's Entry
- Learned of Batista's flight morning of January 1
- Took Santiago de Cuba January 2 (Colonel Rubido ordered no resistance)
- Guevara and Cienfuegos entered Havana same day
- "They had met no opposition on their journey from Santa Clara"
- Castro arrived in Havana January 8 after victory march

### Immediate Aftermath in Havana
- Jubilant crowds; "black and red flag of the July 26 Movement waved on cars and buildings"
- Chaos: looting, vandalism, arson (Sans Souci casino burned)
- Slot machines destroyed
- Sporadic violence killed 13
- Shouts of "Abajo Batista, Viva Fidel"

## Aftermath

### Batista's Exile and Death
- Initial asylum in Dominican Republic under Trujillo
- Denied entry to United States and Mexico
- Eventually settled in Portugal under Salazar (Madeira, then Estoril)
- Condition: "completely abstain from politics"
- Business activities in Francoist Spain
- Died of heart attack August 6, 1973, in Marbella, Spain

### Revolutionary Justice
- Trials of Batista officials and security forces
- Many BRAC/SIM members captured and executed
- Jose de Jesus Castano (BRAC) executed
- Some BRAC agents fled to U.S.; "found their next home within the CIA"
- U.S. Consulate noted executed were "for the greatest part members of military organizations connected with repressive activities such as SIM"

### Counter-Revolutionary Activity
- Dominican Republic became base for anti-Castro plotting
- Force organized under ex-General Jose Pedraza
- Included Cuban exiles from Batista regime

### Long-term Impact
- Cuba transformed into communist state
- U.S.-Cuba relations deteriorated; embargo imposed
- Cold War flashpoint (Bay of Pigs, Cuban Missile Crisis)
- Exodus of middle/upper class Cubans
- Casino industry nationalized; gambling outlawed (October 1960)
- Lansky and organized crime expelled

### Family Aftermath
- Marta Fernandez Miranda de Batista survived until October 2, 2006
- Four of five children with Marta survived (Carlos Manuel died 1969 of leukemia)
- Three children from first marriage to Elisa Godinez

## Scholarly Debate

### The "Multiple Batistas" Problem
- Historians distinguish between 2-3 different Batistas:
  1. Revolutionary/dictator of the 1930s
  2. Democratic leader of the 1940s
  3. Brutal dictator of the 1950s
- "Attributing the same motives and goals to all three Batistas amounts to superficial and deterministic scholarship"
- "A central point of this analysis is the need to study leading political figures within their historical context"

### Role in 1933 Sergeants' Revolt
- Debate over whether Batista was minor figure or leader
- "Batista was not originally the leader of the movement"
- Some scholarship challenges view that he was mere tool of U.S. representative Sumner Welles

### Death Toll Controversy
- JFK's 20,000 claim vs. Batista's denial ("Formidable Lie")
- Bohemia documented 746; historians commonly cite 4,000
- 20,000 would be 1 in 300 Cubans - questioned as statistically improbable
- Havana morgue records provide documentary evidence for at least 700 torture deaths

### Revolutionary Narratives vs. Historical Reality
- "Major scholars have been influenced by revolutionary narratives"
- View of first regime often colored by "failures of his second"
- Antonio de la Cova provided "systematic and scrupulously balanced study" dispelling myths
- Some claims about Moncada torture disputed by historians

### U.S. Complicity Debate
- Extent of U.S. knowledge and support for repression
- CIA role in training BRAC
- Arms embargo decision: genuine shift vs. too little too late

### First Period Assessment
- Generally viewed more favorably than second period
- 1940 Constitution considered genuinely progressive
- 1944 election was legitimate democratic transfer
- Question of whether patronage-based rule qualifies as dictatorship

## Confidence Assessment
**Overall**: Medium-High

**Notes**:
- **Well-documented**: Basic biographical facts, dates of coups and revolution, existence and general activities of BRAC/SIM, organized crime connections, 1940 Constitution, U.S. arms embargo
- **Reasonably documented**: Moncada attack and aftermath, methods of torture and repression, key political figures
- **Contested/Uncertain**:
  - Death toll estimates vary enormously (746 to 20,000); true figure likely between 2,000-5,000
  - Details of specific torture cases at Moncada (some historians question accounts)
  - Exact extent of U.S. knowledge of human rights abuses
  - Precise amounts of Batista's personal fortune (estimates range $100-700 million)
- **Bias considerations**: Cuban government sources tend to emphasize atrocities; exile sources may minimize; U.S. sources shaped by Cold War context
- **Recommended further research**: Antonio de la Cova's academic work on Moncada; declassified CIA documents; Bohemia magazine archives
